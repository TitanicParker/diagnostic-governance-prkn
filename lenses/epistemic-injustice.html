Epistemic injustice

Recorded but not governing

This lens asks what happens when a patient’s testimony is repeatedly documented but denied interpretive authority.

Working thesis: the patient was not simply unheard. The more subtle problem is that the patient’s account was heard, recorded, and then subordinated to a governing frame.

The case is especially useful because the patient’s reports of foot pain, dystonic features, medication response, and diagnostic unease appear in the record. The ethical question is why those reports did not become governing evidence.

Epistemic injustice here is not only a matter of tone or bedside manner. It concerns the status of the patient as a knower inside the medical record.

Key questions

  • When does documentation become containment?
  • Can testimony be respected socially while being neutralised clinically?
  • What happens when a patient is treated as insightful but not authoritative about the organising symptom?
  • How does a diagnostic frame control the credibility of later patient reports?
