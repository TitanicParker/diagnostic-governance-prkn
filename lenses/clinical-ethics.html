Clinical ethics

Patient-facing records and the ethics of explanation

This lens examines autonomy, disclosure, clinical uncertainty, therapeutic caution, and the ethical burden created when a record is written partly for the patient.

The central ethical fork is the patient-facing discharge correspondence. Once a record states that it is partly for the patient, it becomes more than professional correspondence. It becomes an instrument of understanding.

The question is not whether the patient received a long and detailed letter. The question is whether the letter disclosed the architecture of the decision: what was known, what remained uncertain, what had been deferred, and what the diagnostic label would be allowed to do.

Key questions

  • Was uncertainty communicated as uncertainty, or absorbed into a settled frame?
  • Was levodopa explained only as therapy, or also as diagnostically meaningful?
  • Was the initiating foot-centred problem resolved, superseded, or merely displaced?
  • Did the patient receive information without genuine epistemic agency?
