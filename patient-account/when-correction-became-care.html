Patient account

When Correction Became Care

This essay is written from the edge between record and memory.

Reader note: this is a patient-authored account. It is not offered as a finding against any person or institution. It does not ask the reader to treat patient memory as identical to documentary proof. It does not allege malice or require the claim that the correct answer was obvious in 2017.

It begins from something simpler.

The patient had only ever wanted to be well.

He entered the medical system because his body was failing him. He did not set out to build a case, challenge an institution, become a procedural actor, learn regulatory language, reconstruct medical chronology, or contest the authority of a clinical record. He wanted help. He wanted to walk. He wanted the pain in his feet to be understood. He wanted the thing that had brought him into care to be made better, or at least made intelligible.

That fact must govern the archive.

The documentary case rests on a small number of load-bearing materials: the GP referral, the discharge summary and associated consult correspondence, the DAT scan addendum, the longitudinal neurology notes, and the official response to the patient’s complaint of 19 March 2025. Those documents are the administrative and bureaucratic case. They are enough to show the structure: a foot-centred referral; a Parkinsonian frame applied under uncertainty; a discharge record that preserved atypicality while creating finality; later notes in which the feet kept returning; and a 2025 institutional response that acknowledged suffering while preserving the diagnostic architecture.

External to that documentary structure is memory.

One item matters especially. The patient remembers a bedside protest near discharge: a real-time objection that the problem which had brought him into hospital — the feet, the walking, the unresolved bodily signal — had not been answered. That remembered protest is not present in the notes. Within this project, that absence is called the omission.

The omission should be handled carefully. It is not the foundation of the case. The foundation is documentary. The omission is a pressure point where patient memory and official record diverge. Its importance is not that it proves misconduct. Its importance is that, if the patient’s memory is accurate, then the discharge record fails to capture the final patient-side attempt to restore the initiating problem to the centre before administrative closure took effect.

The patient remembers that this occurred not in a private, sealed exchange, but in a teaching context. That matters.

The remembered scene is not merely “patient complained.” It is more specific and more consequential than that. The patient remembers being the subject of a bedside teaching interaction on St. Anthony’s Ward, in the presence of four medical trainees, under the supervision of hospital clinical education. He remembers raising the concern that his core presenting symptom — disabling bilateral foot pain — had not been addressed. He remembers requesting that one of the students fetch the supervising neurologist. He remembers saying, in the presence of the trainees:

“So are you telling me you’re going to bring me in here, give me the full NCT, and send me out on bald tires?”

That sentence matters because it was not a technical objection. It was a patient’s bodily metaphor. It said: you have inspected the machine, but you are sending it out on the same damaged contact points by which it meets the ground. It said: the system may have run tests, but the thing that makes walking possible has not been made safe. It said: the feet have not been answered.

The remembered response is equally important, but must be handled with the same restraint. The patient remembers the consultant’s response, paraphrased, as:

“It’s as easy for you to get a podiatry appointment as it is for me.”

Again, the archive should not overclaim. It should not say that the sentence is proven as a verbatim clinical note. It should say that this is the patient’s remembered version of the exchange. But it should also say why the remembered content is structurally significant.

Because if the patient’s memory is accurate, the protest was not only clinically relevant. It was educationally relevant. It occurred in front of trainees. It involved a patient identifying a mismatch between the reason for admission and the plan for discharge. It involved the initiating signal — the feet — being returned to the centre by the person whose body had produced it. And the remembered response displaced the issue toward podiatry.

That is the really real part.

Not melodrama. Not accusation. A scene.

A patient in a hospital bed or ward space, near the end of an admission. A teaching interaction. Students present. A patient trying to say that the core problem has not been solved. A metaphor about an NCT and bald tyres. A response remembered as locating the solution in podiatry access. A discharge record that later carries the diagnosis forward but does not visibly carry that protest.

This is why the omission matters.

The patient did not need technical language to make that protest meaningful. He did not need to know about diagnostic discriminators, levodopa response, administrative foreclosure, or record-mediated harm. Those are later analytical terms. At the bedside, the matter was simpler. He had come in because he could not walk properly, because his feet were central to the failure, and because his body was telling him that the explanation being installed had not resolved the problem that had brought him there.

The teaching context makes this more than a missing anecdote. Teaching is not informal atmosphere. In a university-affiliated hospital, teaching is governance. Trainees observe not only signs and diagnoses, but professional posture. They see what counts as a patient objection. They see what happens when a patient resists closure. They see whether a patient’s embodied concern becomes a reason to pause, escalate, re-examine, document, or simply move the issue sideways.

Care and education were present together.

That is why the later Article 15 requests mattered. The patient was not simply asking for “more notes.” He was asking whether the institutional traces of that teaching interaction existed: clinical notes, trainee records, logbooks, ward-round records, consultant entries, trainee annotations, governance documents, education documents, or rosters identifying students present on the case during that week.

That was not a fishing expedition in the ordinary sense. It was a targeted question about the institutional life of a remembered event.

If the interaction occurred as remembered, then it sat at the intersection of several duties: clinical care, patient record-keeping, consultant supervision, medical education, data protection, and institutional accountability. The patient was not asking the hospital or university to accept his memory as documentary proof. He was asking the relevant controllers to search for the documentary environment in which such a memory would have occurred.

That distinction matters.

The hospital, as controller or institutional holder of clinical and ancillary data, had one kind of responsibility. The university, as independent controller for university-governed teaching, supervision, and academic processing, had another. They were not interchangeable. A bedside teaching interaction in a university-affiliated hospital does not belong only to the patient chart. It may also belong to the teaching system: student placement, supervision, rosters, logbooks, case-based discussion, reflective notes, governance documentation, academic oversight.

The patient therefore approached both structures.

The hospital response, as later analysed in the project materials, treated the relevant categories as absent or unavailable. It supplied some clinical records but refused or denied holding personal data for categories such as student involvement records, student records, ward rounds, governance or education documents, and trainee assignment records. It also pushed podiatry records outside the hospital service and toward community healthcare. In that response, absence became the answer.

That is procedurally important.

The hospital did not say, in the relevant categories, “we hold records but they contain third-party data and must be redacted.” It did not appear to engage the Article 15(4) redaction logic in the way the patient had requested. Instead, the institutional posture became: no personal data relating to your request is held.

The university path then became a second and distinct data-controller pathway. The patient wrote to the university as an independent controller for teaching, supervision, and academic processing. That request asked for access, rectification, and restriction in relation to any university-held personal data arising from the ward-based teaching interaction. The logic was direct: if the hospital denied relevant data existed in its sphere, the university’s teaching-governance sphere had to be addressed separately.

The university-controller episode then became its own event. As framed in the later complaint, the issue was complete non-engagement: no response within the statutory timeframe, no extension notice, no refusal, no confirmation of restriction, and no clarification as to whether relevant personal data existed or had been processed. The patient’s position was that the university did not even perform the basic act of saying whether it held data, should hold data, or had processed data relating to the teaching interaction.

This is what made the responses incompatible in practical effect.

One institutional pathway treated absence as definitive. Another, as the patient framed it, did not clarify the existence or non-existence of teaching-related data at all. The hospital’s response narrowed the field. The university’s silence left the academic field unresolved. Between them, the patient was left with memory on one side and institutional non-confirmation on the other.

That is not proof that the memory is right.

It is proof that the memory could not be meaningfully tested through ordinary access routes.

That is the deeper problem.

The patient asked, in effect: if my remembered protest occurred in a teaching context, what does the institution hold that would show the educational, supervisory, or governance trace of that event?

The hospital answer, in effect, closed down multiple categories.

The university answer, in effect, did not arrive.

And so the omission became more than a remembered absence from the discharge note. It became a continuing absence across data systems: no visible record in the clinical discharge account, no accessible teaching trace from the hospital route, no substantive university-controller clarification within the statutory period.

This is why the omission belongs in the essay.

Not as accusation.

As architecture.

Because the remembered protest concerns exactly the issue the documentary record later confirms in other ways: the feet remained central, the patient kept returning to them, and later medication response made the unresolved nature of that signal impossible to dismiss. The patient’s memory says he tried to raise the matter at the moment of discharge. The record does not show that protest. Later records show the feet did not go away.

That is enough to make the omission ethically serious.

By March 2025, the case was no longer only about 2017.

For years, the patient had lived under a diagnostic frame that had shifted from uncertainty into administrative certainty. The original record contained atypicality, young onset, genetic possibility, dystonic-looking gait, severe foot-centred symptoms, and medication caution. Yet the governing label hardened around idiopathic Parkinson’s disease. The feet remained present. The pain remained present. Dystonic features remained present. But the frame that organised the record retained command.

Then Sinemet CR changed the situation.

The effect was not abstract. It was bodily. Foot pain reduced. Walking improved. The intolerable quality of the condition changed. The patient had already been living with pain severe enough to become existential. Later records captured that foot pain was the thing on his mind, that the first steps of the morning could be torture, and that the feet had dominated his lived condition for years.

That moment did not simply bring relief.

It created an obligation.

If a treatment altered the very symptom that had dominated the case from the beginning, then the record could no longer safely remain untouched. The patient was not merely asking whether something had gone wrong years earlier. He was asking how a corrected understanding could be made to govern now. Something needed to be fixed because the unfixed record was still organising care.

This is the ethical meaning of the 2025 sequence.

The patient did not turn to complaint, governance correspondence, GDPR rectification, or restriction because he wanted to become procedural. He turned to them because ordinary routes of correction did not correct the record, and ordinary routes of care remained constrained by the record that required correction.

The formal complaint of 19 March 2025 was the first major attempt to make the record answer. It raised diagnostic anchoring, failure to reassess, prolonged pain, therapeutic contradiction, and the need for independent review, disclosure, explanation, and preservation of records. It did not require a finding of malice. It did not ask the system to pretend the answer had been obvious in 2017. It asked a narrower and more urgent question: how could the governing explanation remain in force once the patient’s response to treatment had undermined its practical adequacy?

The later closure as clinical judgment did not answer that question. It explained the clinical frame from within the clinical frame. It acknowledged aspects of the patient’s account, including the effect of Sinemet on the experience of pain, but preserved the diagnostic architecture. The contradiction was not allowed to govern. It was absorbed.

That distinction matters.

A system can acknowledge suffering and still fail to give suffering corrective power. It can recognise that a patient has endured pain and still leave intact the record structure through which that pain had been interpreted. It can apologise, explain, and close, while leaving the patient in the same practical position: still dependent on the record that had failed to account for him.

The patient’s subsequent work in April 2025 should be understood in that light. The 18 April response, the support request, the ethical notification, and the appointment-pathway warning were not separate eruptions. They were escalating attempts to make correction possible after closure had returned the complaint to the authority of the existing frame.

The patient was trying to create a route where none was functioning.

This is the point at which the remembered omission becomes ethically relevant without being overstated. If the patient did object at the bedside that the feet had not been addressed, then the later 2025 correspondence was not the beginning of protest. It was the return of an old protest in a form the institution could no longer avoid recording. The patient’s memory suggests continuity: from the ward, to the clinic letters, to the complaint, to the data requests, the same point kept returning.

The feet had never been answered.

The record, however, did not preserve that earliest remembered protest. It preserved the diagnosis. It preserved the plan. It preserved the caution. It preserved the professional account of uncertainty. It did not preserve, in any visible way, the patient’s remembered objection that closure was proceeding while the initiating problem remained unresolved.

The archive should not inflate that absence into a conclusion it cannot prove. It should simply let the asymmetry stand.

The official record became authoritative.

The patient’s remembered protest did not.

That is enough.

By late May 2025, the issue was no longer only whether the diagnosis had been accurate. It was whether care could happen while the disputed diagnosis continued to govern. The patient requested treatment for dystonia and described being positioned between untreated dystonia and medication-induced dyskinesia. Primary care did not appear indifferent. Rather, it appeared constrained. The GP’s position was that there was very little he could do other than refer back to neurology.

The bottleneck was not lack of concern in primary care.

It was the inherited hierarchy of the specialist record.

That is downstream harm.

The record did not merely misdescribe the patient upstream; it disabled care downstream.

Once the specialist frame had become authoritative, primary care could not simply act against it. Medication changes required specialist sanction. Botox access required specialist pathway. Levodopa adjustment required neurology guidance. The patient could report benefit, pain, dystonia, and risk, but those reports still had to pass through the authority of the record that had already classified him.

At that point, the patient entered a dangerous practical space. He was not outside medicine, but he was not adequately held by it. He was adjusting, splitting, timing, managing supply, and trying to stabilise a narrow therapeutic situation while waiting for the system to recognise what the body had already made clear.

Self-management became a substitute for coordinated care.

That is not empowerment in any simple sense.

It is labour transferred to the patient.

It is risk transferred to the patient.

It is clinical uncertainty returned to the person least protected from its consequences.

The June and July 2025 data-protection requests must be read against that background. They were not a legal detour away from care. They were an attempt to reach care through the only available mechanism that addressed the governing record itself.

Rectification was used because the problem had become one of accuracy. The record did not merely preserve past statements; it continued to organise present treatment. If the governing data remained incomplete, over-finalised, or misleading, future care would continue to be routed through the wrong structure.

Restriction followed the same logic. If accuracy was disputed, continued reliance on the disputed record became ethically significant. The patient’s concern was not clerical. It was clinical. The disputed record was still deciding what could be done, who could act, and what kind of treatment request was intelligible.

Access then became the instrument for testing the omission. Not proving it by assertion. Testing it through access. The patient asked the hospital for the personal data and institutional documentation that would show whether the remembered teaching interaction had left a trace. He later asked the university, as the teaching controller, to account for its own possible processing. That is exactly what one would do if one were trying to avoid overclaiming. One does not simply say, “this happened and was hidden.” One asks: what records exist, what records should exist, who holds them, who processed them, and why is the remembered event absent from the accessible record?

The patient did not turn to data protection instead of care.

He turned to data protection because care was still being routed through the record that needed correction.

This is where the case becomes more than a diagnostic-error narrative. It becomes a study in what happens when a healthcare record becomes the infrastructure of access, and when a remembered educational encounter becomes relevant to the integrity of that record. The patient may prove, through lived pharmacological response, that something in the inherited explanation is materially incomplete. He may remember having said so at the bedside, in front of trainees, before the record closed. But unless the record changes, the system may continue to behave as though the old explanation remains governing.

Relief, by itself, is not correction.

A medication can reduce pain without changing the file. A patient can walk better while the record still says the same thing. A contradiction can become visible without becoming authoritative. The body can produce evidence, and the archive can still refuse to reorganise around it.

That is why the patient had to fight.

The fight was not for conflict. The fight was not for grievance as an identity. The fight was not for the pleasure of accusation.

The fight was for wellness after wellness had become procedurally inaccessible.

The patient became adversarial because correction was not otherwise available. He was not fighting about the past only. He was fighting because the uncorrected past was still deciding what care he could receive.

That is the line the archive must hold.

The patient had only ever wanted to be well. When the medication response should have brought him closer to being well, it instead exposed the uncorrected structure of the record. When the fight should have been over, the fight began.

The omission is the concentrated image of that whole reversal.

At the moment care, education, and documentation converged, the patient remembers saying: the feet have not been addressed. The remembered response moved toward podiatry. The discharge record carried forward Parkinson’s disease. Years later, the feet returned as the central suffering. Sinemet CR altered them. The complaint asked the record to answer. The closure preserved the frame. The data requests asked whether the teaching moment had left institutional traces. The hospital narrowed. The university, on the patient’s account, did not substantively engage.

That is not a dramatic allegation.

It is a devastating sequence.

The ethical difficulty is that no single actor need be cast as malicious for this harm to occur. Primary care can be responsive and still immobilised. A consultant can be sincere and still preserve the frame. A complaints office can apply process and still narrow the question. A data controller can process requests and still fail to make correction clinically effective. A university controller can fail to engage and thereby leave the academic layer unresolved. The harm sits in the arrangement.

The patient was placed in a structure where every route required more precision from him.

He had to explain the clinical contradiction.

He had to preserve the chronology.

He had to distinguish complaint from governance.

He had to separate clinical judgment from data accuracy.

He had to identify the continuing effect of disputed data.

He had to request rectification.

He had to invoke restriction.

He had to request access to teaching-interaction data.

He had to distinguish hospital-controller duties from university-controller duties.

He had to document access-to-care failure.

He had to show that primary care was downstream.

He had to become the mechanism by which the system could see what the system had not corrected.

This is the burden that should not have fallen on him.

The archive exists because recognition failed in ordinary form. The patient became the historian of the case because the record had not been made answerable to him. He became the sorter, sequencer, analyst, and witness because the systems that held the record did not themselves produce the correction that care required.

That does not make the archive merely personal.

It makes it ethically important.

It shows that diagnostic harm does not end when the patient discovers the contradiction. In some cases, discovery begins a second phase of harm: the phase in which the patient must persuade the institution to let the contradiction matter. That phase can be as exhausting as the original error because the patient is no longer only suffering from symptoms. He is suffering from the record’s refusal, incapacity, or delay in becoming corrigible.

The March-to-August 2025 sequence is therefore not administrative background. It is the end-stage clinical event. Sinemet CR revealed that something had to change. The complaint asked for correction. The closure preserved the frame. The dystonia access request exposed downstream paralysis. The GP could not safely govern what the specialist record had not authorised. Data-protection rights became the route by which the patient tried to stop disputed data from continuing to control care. Access rights became the route by which the patient tried to test whether the remembered protest had any institutional trace. The university-controller route became necessary because the remembered scene was not only clinical; it was educational.

This is what “correction became care” means.

Correction was not an afterthought to treatment.

Correction was a condition of treatment.

Until the record could be corrected, qualified, restricted, accessed, or made answerable, the patient remained trapped between what his body had shown and what the system was still prepared to act upon. The record did not merely describe the patient’s condition. It determined the conditions under which help could be given.

That is the unresolved ethical lesson.

Healthcare systems often treat records as documentation of care already delivered. This case shows that records can become determinants of care not yet accessible. When that happens, data accuracy is not bureaucratic. Rectification is not peripheral. Restriction is not procedural excess. Access is not curiosity. They become instruments by which a patient attempts to regain access to reality inside the system.

The remembered omission belongs at the beginning of that arc, but not as proof of wrongdoing. It belongs there as the patient’s earliest remembered version of the same sentence that later became the whole archive:

the thing I came in with has not been answered.

Everything after that is repetition at higher resolution.

The formal complaint says it in administrative language.

The support request says it in human language.

The access-to-care sequence says it in clinical language.

The rectification request says it in accuracy language.

The restriction request says it in reliance language.

The access request says it in access language.

The university-controller complaint says it in teaching-governance language.

The archive says it in ethical language.

But the sentence is the same.

The patient was asking the system to return to the body it had classified before it had fully understood.

He was not fighting because he wanted to fight.

He was fighting because the route to being well now ran through correction.
