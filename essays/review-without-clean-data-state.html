Review Without a Clean Data State

A review cannot resolve a dispute about the record if it first treats the disputed record as its stable ground.

Publication note: this essay does not make a legal finding about any review process. It analyses the patient-safety and data-governance problem created when an internal review relies on records whose accuracy, completeness, or authority is already contested.

Internal review is often treated as the route out of dispute. A concern is raised; the institution reviews the matter; a conclusion is issued. The review is then expected to provide finality.

But finality depends on the condition of the material being reviewed.

If the central allegation is that the record has over-finalised uncertainty, displaced the originating symptom, or preserved an inherited diagnostic frame beyond its evidential authority, then the review cannot simply rely on that same record as neutral ground. The record is not only evidence in the case. It is part of the case.

This is the problem of review without a clean data state.

A clean data state does not mean a perfect record. No clinical archive is perfect. It means that the review body knows which data are contested, why they are contested, what versions or extracts are being used, what omissions are alleged, what later contradictory information exists, and what limits should be placed on reliance. It means the review does not treat disputed data as ordinary background merely because they are institutionally available.

In this case, the move from complaint handling into incident management created an important shift. The matter was no longer only a complaint about diagnostic anchoring or failure to reassess. It became a governance review process, capable of producing an institutional outcome about the adequacy of care. That outcome would itself become part of the patient's record environment.

The difficulty is that, by the time such a review was underway, the patient had already challenged the accuracy and completeness of the underlying data. He had raised Article 16 rectification concerns. He had argued that the record omitted or subordinated the foot-centred presenting problem. He had challenged continued reliance on unqualified diagnostic data. He had later invoked Article 18 restriction, seeking to prevent disputed entries from being processed without qualification while the accuracy dispute remained unresolved.

Those steps matter analytically even before any regulator makes findings. They show that the review did not occur in a neutral documentary environment. It occurred in a contested data environment.

The ethical question is not whether an institution may review its own records. It must be able to do so. The question is what safeguards are required when the reliability of those records is itself one of the matters under review.

Without such safeguards, the review risks circularity. The inherited record frames the question. The review assesses care through that inherited frame. The outcome then confirms that care was appropriate within the frame. But the patient's concern was precisely that the frame had become too powerful to correct.

That is not independent learning. It is record-confirming review.

The problem becomes sharper where the review outcome uses definitive language. A finding that there was no deficit in care does not remain internal. It travels. It can be cited by complaint handlers, data controllers, clinicians, regulators, and future reviewers. It becomes new personal data generated from old personal data. If the old data were disputed, the new conclusion may carry forward the dispute while appearing to settle it.

This is why data provenance matters in patient safety. A serious review should not only ask what the notes say. It should ask what the notes were able to know, what they omitted, what they made central, what they made peripheral, and whether later evidence changes the safety of relying on them unqualified.

The patient's Article 18 position can be understood in that light. Restriction is not merely a bureaucratic obstacle. It is a demand that disputed data stop operating as if uncontested until the dispute has been reconciled or lawfully refused. In a clinical-governance setting, that demand has obvious ethical force. A review based on restricted or disputed data should be transparent about its data sources, assumptions, limitations, and qualifications.

The alternative is dangerous because it allows process to outrun accuracy.

A review may be procedurally real and still epistemically weak. It may convene appropriate personnel, follow a recognised framework, and produce a formal outcome while failing to examine whether the data architecture it inherited was safe to rely upon. The existence of review is not enough. Review must be capable of answering the actual question.

Here, the actual question was not only whether individual decisions could be defended at the time. It was whether a diagnostic frame had acquired administrative authority before unresolved features had been allowed to govern, and whether subsequent processes relied on that frame without reopening it. A review that treats the frame as background may miss the central risk.

The required standard is not perfection. It is explicitness.

A clean review would identify the contested data state. It would say which records were used. It would acknowledge the patient's accuracy and restriction claims. It would distinguish historical clinical opinion from current data completeness. It would explain whether later contradictory material was included, excluded, or treated as outside scope. It would state what the review could and could not conclude because of those limits.

Such a review might still reach the same substantive conclusion. But the conclusion would be ethically different because it would not pretend that the record was uncontested ground.

The larger lesson is that patient-safety review and data governance cannot be kept apart where the alleged harm is record-mediated. If the record organised the care, then the record's accuracy is part of the safety question. If the review relies on the record, then the review inherits the record's unresolved problems unless it actively controls for them.

A review that does not clean, qualify, or disclose its data state may create finality without creating understanding.

And finality built on contested data is not closure.

It is continuation by another name.
