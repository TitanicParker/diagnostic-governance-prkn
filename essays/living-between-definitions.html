Living Between Definitions

This essay examines the lived condition of being governed by overlapping clinical, administrative, bodily, and genetic definitions.

A diagnosis does not stay on paper.

It enters the patient’s life. It changes how he understands his body, how others understand him, how medications are justified, how symptoms are ranked, how future risk is imagined, how work is negotiated, how welfare systems classify him, and how later disagreement is heard.

For that reason, diagnostic uncertainty is not merely a professional problem. It becomes a lived condition. The patient does not wait outside the record until the record is correct. He lives inside whatever definition the system gives him.

This essay is about that condition: living between definitions.

In 2017, Liam entered hospital through a problem that was not yet fully named. The GP referral described a man with bilateral foot issues, an unusual festinant and stiff gait, difficulty initiating movement, increased tone and stiffness, brisk reflexes, possible cogwheeling, impaired dexterity, and deteriorating work capacity. Parkinsonism was queried, but the referral did not close the case around Parkinson’s disease. It preserved the patient as diagnostically open.

That openness matters because it is the last point at which the patient’s condition appears before it is governed by a dominant institutional name. The referral does not yet require him to live as a Parkinson’s patient, an idiopathic Parkinson’s patient, a neuropathic pain patient, a dystonia patient, or a genetic Parkinsonism patient. It says, more modestly: something serious is happening, and it must be explained.

The discharge record then gives the condition a governing name: Parkinson’s disease. It does so while preserving complexity: young-onset Parkinsonism, atypical features, possible genetic considerations, dystonic-looking gait, unresolved foot and leg symptoms, and caution around levodopa.

From the institution’s perspective, this may have been a working clinical frame. From the patient’s perspective, it became a world.

To be told one has Parkinson’s disease at thirty-nine is not simply to receive information. It is to be placed inside a future. The future includes medication choices, deterioration, movement disorder identity, employment risk, state schemes, family understanding, social imagination, and fear. Even if the clinician intends the frame as nuanced, the patient must live its practical consequences.

The problem in this case is that the name did not fully contain the body.

The feet remained outside, or half-inside. They were present in the record but unstable in meaning. Were they corns and calluses? Were they neuropathy? Were they pain magnified by Parkinson’s? Were they dystonia? Were they OFF symptoms? Were they psychological burden? Were they part of the same disease, a separate disease, or a complication created by the interaction between central movement disorder and peripheral stress?

The records do not settle that cleanly. The discharge summary itself records that the leg symptoms, including corns, callouses, and burning, “may have been completely separate” or perhaps secondary peripheral neuropathy, while also noting that this was “not clear.”

That sentence captures the patient’s lived predicament. One part of him is defined declaratively. Another part of him remains conditional. The diagnosis says what he has. The feet say the definition is incomplete.

Living between definitions means living in that gap.

It means being told enough to accept a framework, but feeling each morning that the framework does not fully explain the body you must inhabit. It means understanding yourself as a Parkinson’s patient while the most destructive symptom seems, repeatedly, to exceed the ordinary explanation being offered. It means having your central suffering recorded but not resolved. It means becoming fluent in a diagnosis that still cannot account for the place where your life hurts most.

The longitudinal notes show this with unusual clarity. In 2025, “the one thing” on Liam’s mind is foot pain. The note records that since 2005, “my feet disturb me”; that he hobbles out of bed; that the first 150 footsteps are torture; that the feet are too sore to tie runners; and that once Parkinson’s medications hit his system, the feet feel better from a pain perspective.

This is not a minor symptom living under a major diagnosis. It is the patient’s governing daily reality. The feet decide how the day begins. They decide whether walking is possible. They decide whether the patient’s experience of himself is one of motion or negotiation, spontaneity or calculation.

And yet the clinical frame remains uneasy with them. The same note says Sinemet “should not do anything for pain” and that pain is “not really a direct part of Parkinson’s,” while also acknowledging painful dystonia and probable further relief from increased Sinemet, limited by dyskinesia risk.

That is what it means to live between definitions. The patient experiences a symptom as central. The medication response suggests centrality. The record acknowledges the response. But the diagnostic language cannot quite let the symptom become central without destabilising the inherited structure.

So the patient is left in a divided ontology. His body says one thing. The record says several things. The administrative label says another. The treatment response speaks again. The complaint process then tells him that the matter is clinical judgment.

This is not just confusing. It is corrosive.

A patient can tolerate uncertainty when uncertainty is openly shared and actively governed. What is harder to tolerate is being required to live inside a definition that behaves as settled while the body continues to produce evidence of incompleteness. That condition creates a particular kind of isolation. The patient is not without medical attention. He is not outside the system. He is inside a system whose categories do not fully match him.

That mismatch can be psychologically devastating because it turns self-knowledge into a contested act. Each time the patient says, “the feet are central,” he is not merely reporting a symptom. He is challenging an architecture. Each time the record absorbs the report without reorganising around it, the patient learns that his own body can be acknowledged without being believed in the relevant way.

The harm is not only that pain persists. It is that pain persists under a definition that cannot adequately answer it.

The 2025 complaint correspondence shows the moment when living between definitions becomes explicit protest. Liam’s complaint states that his presenting foot pain began in 2005; that it was consistently attributed to peripheral neuropathy, likely alcohol-related; that the working diagnosis did not materially change; that Sinemet CR brought immediate and sustained relief; and that this raised the question of why the possibility had not been explored earlier. He explicitly says he makes no presumption of malice and frames the issue as systemic: diagnostic anchoring, confirmation bias, and deference to hierarchy potentially overriding patient experience and clinical curiosity.

The language is significant. The patient is not simply saying, “I disagree.” He is trying to describe what it has meant to live under a frame that never fully accounted for his experience. He is trying to make the institution see that a diagnostic category can become harmful when it manages contradiction instead of reopening inquiry.

The response, again, is not crude dismissal. Dr Monaghan’s March 2025 letter expresses sincere regret, accepts that he did not understand this was how Liam felt, and explains the clinician’s belief that Liam has idiopathic Parkinson’s disease. It acknowledges that Sinemet helps the experience of pain while maintaining that Parkinson’s may magnify pain from other causes and that levodopa escalation must be cautious in a younger person because of dyskinesia and related risks.

The response is humane. But it also demonstrates the patient’s predicament. The inherited definition remains intact. The patient’s contradictory experience is accepted as experience, but not as a reason to change the architecture. The institution can regret the suffering while preserving the frame that made the suffering difficult to reinterpret.

That is the moral tension of the case.

The patient is not simply asking to be believed emotionally. He is asking for his lived experience to have structural consequences. He is asking that the facts he has carried in his body — the feet, the pain, the medication response, the mismatch, the years of recurrence — be permitted to alter the record’s understanding of him.

In 2026, St Vincent’s provides a new definition. The genetic confirmation letter states that Liam has two pathogenic variants in the Parkin gene and that his Parkinsonism and dystonia are due to compound heterozygous mutations in the Parkin gene.

This letter does not erase the previous years. It does not mean that earlier clinicians should have known the full genetic answer. It does something different: it gives causal language to a reality the body had long been trying to express. It confirms that the story was not adequately captured by a simple idiopathic frame. It brings dystonia into the causal centre. It makes the feet newly legible without making them newly real.

The patient had been living with that reality before the record could name it.

That is one of the deepest forms of medical loneliness: to inhabit the correct body before the correct explanation exists, and then to be governed by an incomplete explanation as though it were sufficient.

This is why the essay must not become a triumphalist “final answer” piece. Genetic confirmation is not a victory in any simple sense. It is clarification after years of living between definitions. It may bring relief, but also retrospectively illuminates the cost of the earlier mismatch. It allows the patient to say, not “I knew the gene,” but “the body was not lying.”

The difference matters. The patient did not need to know PRKN/PARK2 in 2017 to know that the feet were central. He did not need genetic vocabulary to know that the explanation being offered did not fully reach the place where the disorder lived. He did not need to out-diagnose the clinicians. He needed the record to remain open enough for his recurring bodily knowledge to matter.

The concept of “living between definitions” also helps avoid a simplistic binary between correct and incorrect diagnosis. Liam did have Parkinsonism. Later confirmation did not say there was no Parkinsonism. It said Parkinsonism and dystonia were due to Parkin mutations. The problem was not mere naming versus misnaming. It was an incomplete naming that governed too powerfully.

This is subtler and more ethically important. A partially correct definition can still injure if it excludes the feature by which the patient’s life is most governed. A broad label can be true enough to open care and false enough to obscure the relevant mechanism. A diagnosis can be clinically plausible and still administratively overconfident. The patient can be “inside” the right disease family while still living under the wrong explanatory architecture.

That is why the title matters. The patient did not live simply outside diagnosis. He lived between definitions.

Between Parkinson’s and Parkinsonism.

Between idiopathic and genetic.

Between pain and dystonia.

Between foot pathology and central motor disorder.

Between being heard and being governed by what was heard.

Between treatment caution and diagnostic deferral.

Between patient-facing explanation and actual epistemic agency.

Between being copied into the letter and being able to alter the structure the letter created.

This condition is not abstract. It affects the daily body. It affects whether the patient trusts morning sensations, whether he believes medication response is meaningful, whether he sees pain as evidence or burden, whether he interprets his own protest as rational, whether he feels permitted to challenge the record, whether he believes the institution can correct itself.

A medical record does not merely define a condition. It can define the patient’s relationship to himself.

If the record says the most central symptom is secondary, the patient must either accept that demotion or resist the record. If he accepts it, he may become alienated from his own body. If he resists it, he may become alienated from the system that controls his care. That is an impossible position. It forces the patient to choose between institutional intelligibility and embodied truth.

This is why the case has significance beyond neurology. It belongs to narrative medicine because it shows how a person’s life can be organised by an explanatory story that is too narrow. It belongs to epistemic injustice because the patient’s account is preserved but not authorised. It belongs to health-data ethics because the record’s categories continue to govern long after their uncertainty should have remained visible. It belongs to diagnostic-error research because it shows how partial fit can be more dangerous than obvious misfit.

The tragedy is not that the system had no language. It had too much language, but the wrong hierarchy.

It had Parkinson’s disease. It had atypicality. It had neuropathy. It had dystonia as a possibility. It had pain. It had medication caution. It had genetic discussion. It had podiatry. It had alcohol context. It had DAT scan language. It had annual follow-up. It had apology. It had complaint classification. It had all the pieces needed to avoid overconfidence.

But the patient still had to live inside the overconfidence.

The patient was not simply misnamed; he was required to live inside the consequences of a name that could explain him only by leaving part of him outside itself.

That is the ethical centre of living between definitions. The harm is not exhausted by delayed precision. It includes the years in which the patient’s lived reality had no stable authorised place. The feet were too central to ignore, but not central enough to govern. Dystonia was too present to vanish, but not present enough to reorganise. Genetics was plausible enough to mention, but not enough to prevent the idiopathic frame from hardening. Sinemet response was strong enough to record, but not enough to force structural reconsideration.

To live in that space is to become divided by the record.

A good archive must repair that division, at least narratively. It must not pretend that all was obvious from the start. It must not weaponise hindsight. But it must restore the patient’s continuity. It must show that the feet, the pain, the gait, the dystonia, the medication response, and the eventual genetic confirmation are not scattered facts. They are chapters in one embodied history.

The patient lived that history before the record could integrate it.

The task now is to write it in a way that finally does.
