Information-Rich but Agency-Poor Disclosure

This essay examines how detailed, sincere disclosure can remain ethically incomplete when it does not equip the patient to understand the architecture of diagnostic uncertainty and administrative consequence.

The record in this case is not thin.

That is what makes it ethically important. A weak archive would be easy to criticise. A sparse record, a silent discharge, a dismissive note, an obvious refusal to engage: these would support a simpler story. But this is not that story. The documents are detailed. They are often careful. They contain nuance, explanation, caution, and patient-facing language. They repeatedly preserve the patient’s account. They show time spent, thought applied, and an effort to make the clinical picture intelligible.

The problem is therefore not informational poverty.

The problem is agency poverty.

Liam was given information. He was not given enough structural power to understand what the information meant for his ability to contest, revisit, or reopen the diagnostic frame.

This distinction matters because modern healthcare often treats disclosure as an information problem. If the patient is told enough facts, copied into letters, given explanations, and warned of risks, then disclosure appears ethically adequate. But information and agency are not the same thing. A patient can receive many facts and still not know which decision points matter. He can be told what doctors think without being told what would make them think again. He can be told why treatment is being deferred without being told that the deferral also preserves diagnostic uncertainty. He can be given a long record and still not be equipped to act within it.

This is the signature of information-rich but agency-poor disclosure.

The 2017 correspondence appears, on its face, unusually inclusive. The clinical letter was long and patient-facing. It recorded extensive history, symptoms, findings, differential possibilities, medication plans, and future concerns. It documented young-onset Parkinsonism, atypical features, genetic possibilities, dystonic gait appearance, levodopa caution, and the plan to avoid dopamine-based therapy for as long as possible because of the patient’s age and the risk of dyskinesias and impulse-control problems.

This is not a closed, blunt, paternalistic letter in the obvious sense. It explains. It contextualises. It anticipates. It tries to make the clinical reasoning legible.

Yet legibility is not the same as agency.

The record explains why levodopa or dopamine agonist treatment was being delayed. It frames the delay as a long-term management strategy in a young patient. That may have been clinically reasonable. But the ethical question is whether the patient was made to understand the diagnostic significance of that delay. Was levodopa explained only as a powerful future treatment, or also as a principal discriminator whose withholding meant that one important route of clarification would not be used before the frame hardened?

That is not a minor distinction. If the patient understands levodopa only as treatment, he may reasonably accept deferral as prudence. If he understands levodopa also as a diagnostic discriminator, he may ask different questions: what does delaying it mean for certainty? How will the diagnosis remain open? What will prevent the working frame from becoming fixed? What signs would trigger reassessment? How will my foot-centred presenting problem be resolved if the main clarifying therapy is withheld?

Those are agency questions.

The patient was given an explanation of what the clinicians intended, but not a working map of what their intentions would prevent him from contesting.

The DAT scan addendum deepens the problem. It reports that the DAT scan was abnormal “as was expected” and frames the result within the existing Parkinsonian interpretation.

Again, information is given. The patient is told about the scan. But the record does not appear to make clear the limits of what the scan could settle, or the fact that it was being incorporated after diagnostic and administrative steps had already begun. The result is disclosed, but its relationship to the decision architecture is not fully exposed.

A patient reading “abnormal as expected” is invited into confirmation. He is not necessarily invited into methodological critique. He may not know that a DAT scan supports dopaminergic deficit without resolving all relevant distinctions between idiopathic Parkinson’s, genetic Parkinsonism, dystonia-dominant presentations, and other parkinsonian syndromes. He may not know what the scan cannot answer about his feet. He may not know that the scan is reinforcing a frame rather than governing its formation.

Disclosure, here, becomes confirmation-shaped.

That matters because disclosure is not only about transmission. It is about orientation. The ethical aim is not merely that the patient hear the clinician’s reasoning. It is that the patient be positioned to understand the status of that reasoning, its limits, its contingencies, and its consequences.

In the longitudinal record, Liam’s experience remains vividly disclosed back to him. The 2025 note preserves his own language: “my feet disturb me,” “murder in the feet,” the first 150 footsteps as torture, the feet too sore to tie runners, the sense that medications bring relief, and the statement that “I won’t live with it” gives words to the situation.

This is high-information documentation. It is not emotionally sterile. It is not indifferent to suffering. It captures the patient’s bodily reality with unusual force.

But agency requires more than being vividly recorded. It requires that recorded experience have a route to alter the structure of care. Otherwise the patient’s words become beautifully preserved but practically contained.

The same note acknowledges that once Sinemet hits his system, the feet feel better from a pain perspective. It then states that Sinemet should not do anything for pain, that pain is not really a direct part of Parkinson’s, but that painful dystonia can occur, and that increasing Sinemet would probably provide some further relief while risking dyskinesias.

The patient is informed of complexity. But what agency does that complexity give him? Does it create a review trigger? Does it reopen the 2017 architecture? Does it change the status of the original foot-centred signal? Does it move the case toward a movement-disorder re-evaluation? Or does it function as another explanatory layer inside the inherited frame?

This is the repeated pattern. The record provides information, but the information does not necessarily create power.

The March 2025 complaint demonstrates the patient’s later recognition of this problem. Liam writes that he submits the complaint with respect for clinical complexity but seeks accountability, transparency, and learning. He states that his presenting symptom was sharp, pressure-sensitive foot pain beginning in 2005; that it was consistently attributed to peripheral neuropathy, likely alcohol-related; that Sinemet CR produced immediate and sustained relief; and that this raised the question why the possibility had not been explored earlier. He requests independent review, full disclosure, written explanation, and preservation of records.

This complaint is not a rejection of information. It is a demand for agency over information. The patient is not saying “you never told me anything.” He is saying, in effect: the explanation I lived under did not give me a meaningful path to challenge the frame that governed my life.

The institutional response then classifies the complaint as relating solely to clinical judgment and forwards it to the consultant neurologist for response.

That classification compounds the agency problem. A patient who asks for structural review of diagnostic anchoring and failure to reassess is routed into the authority of the original clinical frame. The complaint pathway gives him a response, but not necessarily the kind of review his concern required. Once again, information is supplied; agency remains limited.

Dr Monaghan’s response is careful and personal. It expresses sincere regret, says he did not understand this was how Liam felt, accepts his account as his experience, explains the belief that Liam has idiopathic Parkinson’s disease, and gives reasons for cautious levodopa escalation in a younger person. It states that Sinemet had long been understood to help Liam’s pain experience and that the reluctance to increase treatment quickly was tied to dyskinesia and other risks.

This is not no response. It is a substantial response. But it also illustrates the distinction between answer and agency. The patient receives a clinician’s explanation. He does not necessarily receive an independent reconstruction of how the diagnostic frame acquired finality, whether the unresolved foot-centred signal was adequately governed, whether levodopa’s diagnostic significance had been communicated, or whether the patient-facing record equipped him to understand the consequences of deferral.

The response answers inside the frame. The patient’s concern is about the frame.

That is the crux.

Information-rich disclosure can fail when it leaves the patient dependent on the very architecture he needs to examine. If all explanations are generated from within the dominant frame, the patient receives more and more information without gaining external leverage. The record becomes fuller, but not more corrigible.

Agency, in this context, would have required several things.

First, explicit disclosure of unresolved primacy: the feet brought the patient into the system, and the record should have stated clearly whether they had been neurologically explained, excluded, or remained unresolved.

Second, explicit disclosure of diagnostic discriminators: levodopa should have been explained not only as a treatment being delayed for long-term risk reasons, but as a potential source of diagnostic information whose deferral had consequences for certainty.

Third, explicit distinction between working diagnosis and administrative reliance: if Parkinson’s disease was being used for care planning, LTI certification, and medication access, the record should have made clear whether this was settled diagnosis, functional classification, or provisional access language.

Fourth, explicit revision triggers: the patient should have been told what kinds of later developments would require reassessment. Persistent foot-centred pain? Medication-responsive foot symptoms? Dystonia dominance? Genetic findings? Failure of peripheral explanations? These should not have been left as scattered possibilities.

Fifth, meaningful review architecture: when the patient later raised contradiction, the issue should not have been reduced to clinical judgment without addressing the administrative and epistemic structure by which the original judgment became durable.

These are not demands for perfection. They are safeguards against managed understanding.

Managed understanding is not necessarily manipulative. It may be benevolent. It may arise from a clinician’s desire to reassure, simplify, and guide. But when the patient is living under a frame that controls future care, reassurance is not enough. The patient must be able to see where the frame is vulnerable.

A patient-facing record should therefore disclose not only the story but the load-bearing uncertainties.

The difference can be stated plainly.

A narrative disclosure says: “We think this is Parkinson’s disease, but it is complex, and we are being cautious with powerful medication.”

An agency-preserving disclosure says: “We are using Parkinson’s disease as the working frame, but your foot-centred presentation remains incompletely explained; the treatment we are delaying could also provide diagnostic information; because we are delaying it, we must avoid treating the diagnosis as more final than it is; here is how and when we will revisit the question.”

The first informs.

The second empowers.

The ethical injury in this case lies in the distance between the two.

A public archive should be careful not to turn this into a simplistic consent argument. Consent is part of the landscape, but the deeper issue is epistemic agency. Liam did not simply need to approve or refuse a medication. He needed to understand the architecture of the diagnostic process into which he had been placed. He needed to know what remained unresolved, what was being deferred, and what consequences followed from that deferral.

Without that, he could be cooperative without being truly positioned. He could agree with treatment caution without understanding diagnostic cost. He could accept a patient-facing letter without realising that the letter did not preserve his route to challenge. He could live for years with a record that explained him richly but constrained him silently.

That is why “agency-poor” is the right phrase. It does not deny information. It asks what the information enabled.

Did it enable challenge?

Did it enable recognition of uncertainty?

Did it enable the patient to identify the hinge points?

Did it enable later correction?

Did it prevent provisional diagnosis from becoming administrative destiny?

If the answer is no, then the disclosure was ethically incomplete even if it was detailed and sincere.

The case’s broader lesson is powerful. Patient-facing records are not automatically patient-empowering. They can democratise knowledge, but they can also extend professional framing into the patient’s own self-understanding. They can make the patient feel included while leaving the governing decisions untouched. They can create transparency of content without transparency of power.

A long letter can still be a closed room.

The task of the archive is to show this without unfairness. The clinicians may have intended clarity. They may have acted from caution. They may have believed they were protecting long-term outcome. The record may have been written in good faith. None of that resolves the ethical question.

The question is whether the patient was given enough structural knowledge to understand what was happening to his diagnostic future.

On the documentary record, the answer appears to be no.

He was told a great deal.

But he was not given the key to the architecture.
