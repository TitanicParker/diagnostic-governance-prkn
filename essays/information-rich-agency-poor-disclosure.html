Information-Rich but Agency-Poor Disclosure

The patient was given an explanation of what the clinicians intended, but not a working map of what their intentions would prevent him from contesting.

Publication note: this essay does not suggest that the patient was given no information. It argues that information and agency are different ethical goods.

The record in this case is not thin. That is what makes it ethically important. The documents are detailed, careful, and often patient-facing. They preserve nuance, explanation, caution, and patient language. The problem is therefore not informational poverty.

The problem is agency poverty.

A patient may receive many facts and still not know which decision points matter. He can be told what doctors think without being told what would make them think again. He can be told why treatment is being deferred without being told that the deferral may also preserve diagnostic uncertainty. He can receive a long record and still not be equipped to act within it.

The 2017 correspondence disclosed a great deal: young onset, atypicality, genetic possibility, medication caution, levodopa delay, and the plan for gradual treatment. It treated the patient as intelligent and capable of understanding complexity. But ethical disclosure is not measured only by volume. It is measured by whether the patient is given access to the architecture of the decision.

That architecture included the unresolved foot-centred entry problem. The patient had come into care through the feet. The feet remained unresolved. Levodopa was being deferred. The diagnosis was beginning to acquire administrative life. Those facts needed to be connected explicitly for the patient.

If levodopa is understood only as treatment, deferral may look like prudence. If levodopa is also understood as a diagnostic discriminator, deferral raises different questions: what does delay mean for certainty, how will the working diagnosis remain open, and what will prevent the frame from becoming final before the initiating problem is resolved?

The difference is agency. Narrative disclosure says: here is what we think and why we are cautious. Agency-preserving disclosure says: here is what remains unresolved, here is what we are choosing not to test yet, here is the consequence of that choice, and here is how the diagnosis will remain revisable.

The later record shows why this matters. The feet did not disappear. The patient's language returned: the feet disturbed him, the first steps were torture, medication helped the feet from a pain perspective. The record preserved those reports, but preservation did not itself create a route to correction.

That is the central distinction. Being copied into a record is not the same as being empowered by it. Being quoted is not the same as being given a governing role. A patient-facing record can make the patient feel included while leaving the load-bearing decisions inaccessible.

The March 2025 complaint was an attempt to convert information into agency. It asked for independent review, disclosure, explanation, and preservation. It did not merely ask for another explanation inside the same frame. It asked how the frame had remained in force once therapeutic contradiction emerged.

The closure as clinical judgment did not answer that deeper question. It supplied explanation, but not the kind of structural review the patient was seeking. That is why the disclosure problem continued into governance. Information kept arriving. Agency did not.

The lesson is not that clinicians must overwhelm patients with technical detail. It is that where uncertainty and finality intersect, patients need the key to the structure. They need to know not only what is believed, but what is unresolved, what is being deferred, and what would force the system to look again.

A long letter can still be a closed room.
