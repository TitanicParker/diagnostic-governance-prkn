Apology Without Reopening

This essay examines how apology can acknowledge suffering while leaving the diagnostic architecture that produced or preserved harm intact.

An apology can do two very different things.

It can open a case.

Or it can close one.

The distinction is not always visible in tone. A response may be sincere, personal, even moving, and still leave the underlying structure untouched. It may acknowledge suffering without revisiting the process that produced it. It may regret the patient’s experience while preserving the diagnostic frame through which that experience had already been interpreted.

That is the ethical problem in the 2025 response sequence.

The March 2025 complaint placed the institution under a specific demand. Liam was not merely asking for sympathy. He raised diagnostic anchoring, failure to reassess, therapeutic contradiction, prolonged pain, and the need for independent review, disclosure, written explanation, and record preservation. He expressly avoided alleging malice, framing the issue as systemic: that diagnostic framing, confirmation bias, and hierarchy may have overridden patient experience and clinical curiosity.

This matters because the complaint was not simply a request to be comforted. It was a request to reopen the architecture of explanation. The patient was asking how a frame had persisted, why contradiction had not governed, and what mechanisms existed to prevent similar persistence in other cases.

The response from Dr Monaghan is notable for its tone. It begins with a strong expression of regret: he is “truly very, very sorry” that this has been Liam’s experience; he says he did not understand that this was how Liam felt; he acknowledges pain and discomfort documented in the recent letters; and he expresses sorrow both professionally and personally.

That apology should not be dismissed. It is not perfunctory. It is not cold. It recognises relationship. It recognises that something has gone badly wrong in the patient’s lived experience. It is, in human terms, substantial.

But ethically, the question is not whether the apology has feeling. The question is what the apology does.

Does it reopen the diagnostic sequence?

Does it question the authority of the inherited idiopathic frame?

Does it treat the patient’s therapeutic contradiction as a reason for independent reassessment?

Does it distinguish between regret for experience and correction of the record?

Does it alter the institutional pathway?

On the documentary record, the answer appears to be no.

The apology recognises suffering but preserves the frame. The same letter reaffirms the belief and understanding that Liam has idiopathic Parkinson’s disease. It explains that Parkinson’s can magnify pain from other causes, that Sinemet may relieve the experience of pain, and that the clinician’s caution in increasing Sinemet was based on well-known risks in younger patients, including dyskinesia and other complications.

This is a coherent clinical defence. It may be sincerely held. It may be medically reasoned. But it answers the complaint from inside the diagnostic structure being challenged.

That is the central problem.

The patient’s complaint asks: did the structure become closed too early, and did later evidence fail to reopen it?

The response says, in effect: the structure was clinically reasonable, the pain was acknowledged, Sinemet’s benefit was understood, and caution was justified.

These are not the same question.

The letter contains a revealing formulation. It says that Liam’s Sinemet response had been accepted for quite some time and that reluctance to increase treatment quickly was due to dyskinesia and other risks. It also quotes the 2017 passage about delaying levodopa, including the acknowledgement that “in time to come we may regret that we did not start this earlier.”

That quotation is important because it shows that the hinge was not forgotten. The clinician uses it to defend caution. But from the patient’s perspective, it may do the opposite. It shows that the possibility of future regret was known at the beginning, yet the record and administrative pathway still moved forward in ways that made the diagnostic frame powerful. The apology does not resolve that tension. It reinterprets it as evidence of careful management.

This is how a closed frame defends itself: it converts the very signs of uncertainty into proof of prudence.

Again, this does not require bad faith. The clinician may honestly see the 2017 caution as responsible young-onset Parkinson’s management. The patient may see the same caution as the point where diagnostic uncertainty was disabled while finality proceeded. The ethical issue is that the institutional response adopted one reading without creating an independent process capable of testing the other.

The QPS closure then gives the clinician’s explanation procedural force. The April 2025 closure letter states that the complaint was assessed as relating to matters solely of clinical judgment and was forwarded to the consultant neurologist. It encloses the response and directs the request for medical notes to FOI.

This is the moment apology becomes closure.

The patient has raised a governance problem: diagnostic anchoring, failure to reassess, therapeutic contradiction, and need for independent review. The institution classifies it as clinical judgment, routes it to the clinician whose reasoning is under challenge, and treats the response as sufficient for closure.

The apology is therefore not just a personal gesture. It becomes part of an administrative act. It helps the institution close the complaint without reopening the sequence.

That is why this essay must be careful. It should not say the apology was insincere. It should say something more exacting: sincerity does not determine structural adequacy.

A sincere apology can still leave the patient without remedy.

A sincere apology can still preserve the disputed frame.

A sincere apology can still be institutionally used as closure.

A sincere apology can still fail to answer the question that matters.

Liam’s 18 April response identifies this distinction sharply. He notes that the apology was directed internally to QPS rather than directly to him; that the diagnosis was reaffirmed despite contradictory evidence; that QPS accepted the letter as resolution; and that no alternative interpretation was explored. He writes that the system addressed itself while processing him as the file.

That phrase matters because it captures the asymmetry of institutional apology. The patient’s suffering is acknowledged, but the institutional conversation remains largely self-referential. The apology enters the complaint file. The file is closed. The patient receives the result. But the patient’s account does not structurally govern the next step.

The apology recognised the wound, but left intact the instrument that had made the wound difficult to name.

This is not a minor ethical defect. In cases of diagnostic governance, apology without reopening can deepen harm. The patient is told, in effect, “I am sorry this happened to you,” while the record continues to say, “but our frame remains the correct way to understand what happened.” That can feel less like recognition than containment.

It separates compassion from correction.

The patient receives regret, but not revision.

The patient receives explanation, but not independent examination.

The patient receives acknowledgement of experience, but not acknowledgement that the diagnostic structure may have caused or prolonged that experience.

That distinction is central to patient-safety ethics. Apology is not a substitute for learning. An apology that does not alter inquiry may satisfy an interpersonal duty while failing a governance duty. In a case where the alleged harm concerns diagnostic closure itself, the minimum adequate response is not merely regret. It is reopening or independently reviewing the conditions under which closure occurred.

The later genetic confirmation makes this even sharper, though it must not be used unfairly. The 2026 St Vincent’s letter confirms two pathogenic Parkin variants and states that Liam’s Parkinsonism and dystonia are due to compound heterozygous mutations in the Parkin gene.

That does not prove that the March 2025 response was dishonest or that the genetic answer was knowable in 2017. It does show that the patient’s request for reopening was not frivolous. The frame under challenge was incomplete in precisely the domain that later became decisive: genetic Parkinsonism with dystonia rather than a simple idiopathic frame. The patient had been asking the system to reconsider the architecture before the final causal clarification arrived.

The apology did not create that reconsideration.

A public scholarly archive should therefore analyse the apology as a case study in institutional response. It should ask: when a patient raises documentary contradiction, what counts as an adequate reply? Is it enough for the original clinician to explain his reasoning? Is regret sufficient where the complaint concerns the authority of the reasoning itself? Can a complaint be fairly closed as clinical judgment when the complaint challenges how clinical judgment became administratively final?

These questions move the essay beyond the personal. They make the case useful for clinical governance, patient-safety, and health-data ethics.

The answer should be disciplined. A clinician’s response is relevant. It may be necessary. It may provide context no external reviewer could otherwise understand. But it cannot, by itself, resolve a complaint about whether that clinician’s diagnostic frame became too powerful and too resistant to contradiction. That requires role separation. It requires independent reconstruction of sequence. It requires attention to what was known, what was deferred, what was certified, what was omitted, what later evidence contradicted, and how the institution handled the challenge.

Without that, the complaint process risks becoming a mirror.

The patient challenges the frame.

The frame explains itself.

The institution accepts the explanation.

The file closes.

This is not learning. It is preservation.

Again, preservation may be dressed in humane language. That is why the case is so instructive. Institutional closure does not always sound defensive. It can sound careful, sorrowful, reasonable, even affectionate. It can say “Tom and Liam.” It can say “I am truly sorry.” It can say “I accept Mr Moloney’s account.” It can say “I have no objection to independent review.” Yet unless the pathway actually changes, these sentences do not reopen the case.

They soften closure.

Soft closure is still closure.

The moral burden of an apology depends on what follows it. If apology leads to review, correction, qualification, and learning, it becomes reparative. If apology leaves the record unchanged and the complaint closed, it becomes expressive but not corrective. It may even worsen epistemic harm because it acknowledges enough suffering to make the patient feel seen, while withholding the structural response that would make being seen matter.

That is the precise injury here.

The patient was not asking the clinician to feel bad. He was asking the institution to look again.

The response felt, but did not look again in the required way.

This essay should end by distinguishing three levels of institutional response.

The first level is emotional recognition: “I am sorry this was your experience.”

The second is explanatory defence: “Here is why the decisions were made.”

The third is structural reopening: “Here is how we will independently examine whether those decisions acquired too much authority before uncertainty was resolved.”

The 2025 response contains the first two. The patient was seeking the third.

That gap is the ethical meaning of apology without reopening.
