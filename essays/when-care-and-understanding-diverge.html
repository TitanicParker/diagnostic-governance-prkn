When Care and Understanding Diverge

This essay considers the ethical difficulty of cases in which care is continuous, sincere, and documented, yet the patient remains incompletely understood by the explanatory structure governing that care.

A patient can be cared for and misunderstood at the same time.

That is one of the hardest ethical facts in medicine because it resists the usual moral categories. It does not fit the simple story of neglect. It does not require indifference. It does not require cruelty. It may coexist with effort, kindness, diligence, explanation, and years of follow-up.

This case matters because it shows that care and understanding are not the same achievement.

The record contains abundant evidence of care. There were referrals, admission, investigation, correspondence, medication planning, counselling, follow-up, detailed longitudinal notes, attempts to explain risk, and later expressions of regret. The patient was not simply abandoned outside the medical system. He was inside it. He was being seen, written about, copied into correspondence, treated, reviewed, and repeatedly interpreted.

Yet the archive also shows a persistent failure of fit. The feet remained central to the patient’s lived experience. Dystonia-like features appeared and reappeared. Medication response complicated the inherited explanation. Genetic possibility was present early and later became causally significant. The record preserved much of this material, but the governing understanding did not fully reorganise around it.

That is the divergence: clinical care continued while explanatory understanding lagged behind.

The divergence is ethically difficult because ordinary clinical morality often assumes that care carries understanding with it. If a clinician spends time, writes carefully, follows up, explains medication risks, and expresses concern, then it is tempting to say that the patient has been understood. But these are different things. A person may be attended to without the structure of their suffering being grasped. A body may be examined without its governing signal being given authority. A record may be detailed without being rightly organised.

Care is not only a matter of contact. Understanding is not only a matter of information. Understanding requires that the explanatory frame remain answerable to the patient’s recurring reality.

In the earliest record, the patient entered specialist medicine through a foot-centred gait disturbance. The referral described bilateral foot issues, abnormal gait, movement initiation difficulty, increased tone, brisk reflexes, possible cogwheeling, deteriorating dexterity, and work-threatening functional decline. The question was open: Parkinsonian or other underlying diagnosis. That openness was ethically important because it preserved the relation between the feet, gait, tone, and neurological signs as the problem to be explained.

The later records show substantial care, but they also show how the explanatory frame became increasingly dominant. Parkinson’s disease, and later idiopathic Parkinson’s disease, organised medication strategy, caution around levodopa, occupational understanding, certification, annual review, and later institutional response. The patient’s foot-centred suffering continued to appear, sometimes vividly, but it did not consistently govern the frame.

It is possible, therefore, to say two things at once: the patient was cared for, and the patient was not fully understood.

Those statements do not cancel each other. Their coexistence is the ethical terrain.

This matters because the language of care can unintentionally shield misunderstanding from scrutiny. A system may point to the amount of care provided: the letters, appointments, tests, plans, prescriptions, and apologies. All of these are relevant. None should be dismissed. But they do not answer the separate question of whether the care was organised around an adequate understanding of the patient’s condition.

A person can receive a great deal of medicine and still remain misrecognised by the medicine they receive.

The later notes make this especially clear. The patient’s words about the feet were preserved in unusually vivid form: the feet disturbed him, the first footsteps were torture, the morning began with pain, Parkinson’s medication brought relief. These details are not the marks of a record that heard nothing. They are the marks of a record that heard much. The ethical question is what the hearing changed.

If hearing does not alter the hierarchy of explanation, it may become a form of care without understanding. The patient is allowed to speak. The clinician records the speech. The record becomes fuller. But the governing structure remains substantially intact.

This is not a condemnation of clinical effort. It is a more demanding account of clinical responsibility. Medicine owes patients more than activity, more than sincerity, and more than explanation. It owes them an active discipline of revisability: the willingness to let the patient’s recurring reality disturb the frame under which care is being provided.

Care without revisability can become paternalistic even when it is gentle.

This is particularly important in chronic, complex, and diagnostically uncertain illness. A single consultation may reasonably proceed under provisional assumptions. But a long relationship creates a different obligation. Over time, recurrence becomes evidence. Persistent mismatch becomes data. A symptom that repeatedly escapes explanation should not remain a peripheral inconvenience simply because the central diagnosis has become administratively durable.

The patient, in such a case, is not only reporting symptoms. He is bearing longitudinal knowledge.

That knowledge is not infallible. Patients can misinterpret their own bodies. They can form theories that do not hold clinically. They can connect events in ways that later prove mistaken. But the patient remains the only witness who lives the entire temporal sequence from inside the body. That continuity has epistemic value. It should not be reduced to preference, distress, or dissatisfaction.

The divergence between care and understanding becomes most harmful when the patient has no effective route to make that longitudinal knowledge govern. If the patient’s account is recorded but not structurally consequential, he is placed in a paradox. He is visible enough to be documented, but not authoritative enough to change the terms of interpretation.

That paradox can deepen suffering. It means the patient is not only living with symptoms. He is living with the knowledge that the symptoms have been described, preserved, and still not allowed to reorganise care. The harm is not silence. It is the exhaustion of speaking into a structure that can absorb speech without becoming answerable to it.

This is why apology, though important, cannot by itself resolve the divergence. An apology may recognise pain. It may express regret. It may acknowledge that the clinician did not understand how the patient felt. But if it does not reopen the structure by which the patient was understood, the apology remains within the same gap. It cares about the wound without necessarily changing the account that made the wound hard to name.

In this sense, understanding is not a sentiment. It is a governance practice.

To understand a patient is not merely to feel concern, write careful notes, or explain the current plan. It is to keep the explanatory structure accountable to the patient’s lived and recorded reality. It is to ask whether the diagnosis still fits the symptom that keeps returning. It is to distinguish treatment caution from diagnostic closure. It is to mark uncertainty where it matters. It is to create review points when contradiction appears. It is to prevent the record from becoming more certain than the body allows.

The 2026 genetic confirmation gives this divergence its sharpest form. It does not prove that every earlier decision was unreasonable. It does not license a simplistic retrospective claim that the full answer should have been known in 2017. But it does show that the patient’s persistent sense of mismatch was not merely emotional resistance to a diagnosis. The body was pressing against an incomplete explanatory structure. The later language of Parkin-related Parkinsonism and dystonia made legible what the earlier frame had not adequately governed.

That later clarity should not be used to erase the difficulty of the earlier medicine. It should be used to ask a better ethical question: how should care behave when understanding may be incomplete?

The answer is not paralysis. Care must continue. Symptoms must be treated. Working diagnoses must be used. Practical decisions must be made. But care under incomplete understanding must remain visibly provisional in the places where provisionality matters. It must carry its uncertainty into the record, into certification, into treatment rationale, into patient-facing explanation, and into complaint response.

Otherwise care becomes too confident in its own benevolence.

This is one of the most important lessons of the project. Harm does not always arrive through absence of care. Sometimes harm emerges through care that cannot recognise the limits of its own understanding. The patient is followed but not reframed. Heard but not reorganising. Supported but not accurately located. Explained to but not structurally empowered.

Clinical ethics needs language for this condition because many serious cases will not look like abandonment. They will look like care. They will contain effort, correspondence, medication, apologies, and professional concern. Their ethical failure will lie not in the absence of attention, but in the persistence of an explanatory structure that attention itself fails to correct.

This distinction also protects fairness. It allows critique without caricature. It does not require saying that clinicians did not care. It allows the harder sentence: they may have cared, and the care may still have been organised by an inadequate understanding.

That is a more painful conclusion, but also a more useful one.

If a system can only recognise harm when care is absent, it will miss harms produced inside care. It will miss patients who are documented but not understood, followed but not reinterpreted, apologised to but not reopened. It will miss the moral difference between doing things for a patient and understanding what those things mean within the patient’s life.

The project therefore contributes a precise claim to clinical ethics and medical humanities: continuity of care is not proof of continuity of understanding.

A patient can be cared for continuously and misunderstood continuously, and the coexistence of those truths is one of the most difficult realities in clinical ethics.

To take that seriously is not to weaken medicine. It is to strengthen it. It asks medicine to become more accountable not only for whether it acted, but for whether its actions remained answerable to the patient’s reality. It asks records to preserve uncertainty with authority. It asks clinicians to treat recurring mismatch as evidence. It asks institutions to reopen frames when care has continued but understanding has failed to keep pace.

That is the ethical demand left by this case.

Care must not be allowed to stand in for understanding.

And understanding must be measured not by how much the system said, but by whether the patient’s reality could still change what the system thought it knew.
