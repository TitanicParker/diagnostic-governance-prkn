Diagnosis as Gatekeeping

This essay examines how a diagnostic label can move from clinical description into administrative and interpretive gatekeeping.

A diagnosis is supposed to open care.

It gives a name to suffering, organises treatment, permits access, explains uncertainty, coordinates clinicians, and allows a patient to enter systems that otherwise remain closed. In that sense, diagnosis is not merely descriptive. It is practical. It does things.

That practical power is usually beneficial. A diagnosis can unlock medication, social protection, specialist pathways, research knowledge, community support, and legitimacy. For many patients, being diagnosed is the moment at which experience stops being private confusion and becomes publicly intelligible.

But that same power creates danger. When a diagnosis is given authority before the uncertainty beneath it has been properly governed, it can stop opening inquiry and begin closing it. It becomes not just a name, but a gate. It determines which symptoms count, which treatments are sensible, which referrals are necessary, which complaints are reviewable, which records are accurate, and which later contradictions can be absorbed without changing the frame.

That is the gatekeeping problem in this case.

The issue is not that Parkinsonism was considered. The 2017 presentation plainly justified serious neurological concern. The GP referral described difficulty initiating movement, increased tone and stiffness in all four limbs, brisk reflexes, possible cogwheeling, abnormal gait, and deteriorating dexterity. Parkinsonian or other underlying diagnosis was queried because the signs were clinically significant and worsening.

Nor is the issue that doctors must never act under uncertainty. Medicine always acts under uncertainty. A working diagnosis is often necessary. The problem arises when a working diagnosis crosses into administrative finality while the unresolved features that made it uncertain remain active.

The July 2017 discharge summary is therefore a pivotal artefact. It declares “Primary Diagnosis: Parkinsons Disease,” while also preserving a record of complexity: young-onset Parkinsonism, atypicality, possible autosomal recessive or idiopathic Parkinson’s disease, other synucleinopathies, Parkinson’s-plus disorders, genetic possibilities, abnormal dystonic-looking gait, and unresolved foot and leg symptoms.

This coexistence matters. The record is not a simple story of certainty. It contains uncertainty. But the diagnosis is placed in the position of administrative command. It becomes the primary label under which the patient is discharged, treated, followed, certified, and understood.

That is the beginning of gatekeeping.

A label becomes a gatekeeper when it no longer merely answers a question, but controls which questions may be asked next. Once the patient is “Parkinson’s Disease,” the foot pain no longer has the same epistemic status it had at referral. It becomes pain in a Parkinson’s patient. The dystonic-looking gait becomes a feature within Parkinsonism. The medication caution becomes long-term Parkinson’s management. The DAT scan becomes confirmation “as expected.” The patient’s later reports become reports inside the frame rather than reasons to revisit the frame.

The DAT scan addendum illustrates this movement. It says the DAT scan was abnormal “as was expected,” with marked findings in both basal ganglia, and that the report says Parkinson’s Disease or Lewy Body disease, though “this is obviously not LBD.”

The wording is revealing. The scan is not presented as an open decision point. It is presented as expected support for a frame already in operation. That does not mean the scan was irrelevant or wrongly obtained. It means that by the time it appears in the correspondence, its function is confirmatory rather than genuinely gate-opening. It reinforces the path rather than reopening the question.

The Long Term Illness certification then gives the diagnostic frame statutory and administrative force. The LTI application certifies that Liam is under care for treatment of “Parkinson’s” and lists medications including Azilect, Procyclidine, vitamins, and Rivotril. The form is stamped by the Neurology Clinic on 09 July 2017.

The significance is not merely bureaucratic. Certification changes the social life of a diagnosis. It allows a name to travel. It enters pharmacy systems, reimbursement systems, general practice records, medication logic, future correspondence, and patient identity. A diagnosis that has been certified is harder to treat as provisional, even if the original clinical reasoning contained provisional elements.

This is why diagnostic gatekeeping is not only clinical. It is administrative.

A clinical hypothesis can remain flexible in the mind of a thoughtful doctor. But once that hypothesis is discharged, certified, copied, coded, reimbursed, and repeatedly relied upon, it develops institutional inertia. It begins to outlive the uncertainty that accompanied its formation. Later doubt must now overcome not only a medical opinion, but a record ecology.

That ecology matters because it shapes care. The diagnosis controls medication strategy. In this case, the record shows long-standing caution around levodopa and dopamine agonist treatment because of young age, dyskinesia risk, impulse-control risks, and the desire to preserve long-term outcome. Those concerns are clinically serious. They cannot be caricatured as indifference. The March 2025 rebuttal explains that Parkinson’s disease management in younger people is difficult, that levodopa can lead to dyskinesias, and that the clinician had been careful and slow in increasing treatment for that reason.

This is precisely why gatekeeping is a subtle problem. The caution is not irrational. It is coherent inside the Parkinson’s management frame. But that is the point: once the frame governs, the risks of treating Parkinson’s in a young person become the dominant logic. The unresolved question of what the feet signify, and whether dopaminergic responsiveness might have diagnostic significance, becomes secondary to the management strategy attached to the label.

The diagnosis also controls symptom interpretation. The 2025 notes record that Sinemet helps the feet from a pain perspective, but explain that Sinemet “should not do anything for pain” and that pain is “not really a direct part of Parkinson’s,” while allowing that dystonia can be painful and that increasing Sinemet would probably bring some further relief, limited by dyskinesia risk.

This is gatekeeping at the level of meaning. The medication response is real enough to be acknowledged, but not strong enough to reorganise the diagnosis. The pain is severe enough to dominate the patient’s mind, but not direct enough to become the organising phenomenon. Dystonia is possible enough to be mentioned, but not central enough to displace the inherited frame. The diagnosis decides what kind of fact each fact is allowed to be.

That sentence is the mechanism: the diagnosis decides what kind of fact each fact is allowed to be.

A foot symptom can be dermatological, neuropathic, dystonic, mechanical, psychological, OFF-related, amplified by Parkinson’s, or partially responsive to medication. The record can hold all those possibilities. But the diagnosis determines which possibility receives governing status. In this case, the inherited idiopathic Parkinson’s frame appears to have retained that status even as the feet kept returning as the patient’s primary lived problem.

The gatekeeping effect extends beyond clinic into complaints handling. Liam’s formal complaint of March 2025 raised concerns about diagnostic anchoring, bias, failure to reassess, prolonged pain, the effect of Sinemet CR, and the need for independent review, disclosure, written explanation, and preservation of records. The complaint explicitly framed the issue as systemic, not malicious.

The institutional closure response of 15 April 2025 then classified the matter as relating solely to clinical judgment and forwarded it to the consultant neurologist for response. It directed the medical-notes request toward FOI and enclosed the clinician’s letter as resolution.

This is diagnosis as procedural gatekeeper. Once the matter is classified as clinical judgment, the complaint is routed away from governance questions about sequence, record authority, administrative finality, and data accuracy. The diagnostic frame becomes protected by the very category that names it as clinical. The patient’s challenge is no longer treated primarily as a question about how uncertainty became final. It becomes a complaint about clinical judgment, answered by the clinician whose judgment is under challenge.

Again, this need not require bad faith. Administrative systems classify complaints. They must decide jurisdiction. They must avoid substituting lay complaint processes for clinical review. But in this case, classification had a powerful effect. It narrowed a governance problem into a clinical-judgment channel. The gate closed around the diagnosis.

The March 2025 Monaghan response shows the same dynamic in more humane form. It apologises sincerely. It says Liam’s experience had not been understood. It accepts his account as his experience. It states there was no objection to independent review, full disclosure, written response, and preservation of records. It also reaffirms the belief that Liam has idiopathic Parkinson’s disease and explains pain through Parkinson’s magnification of symptoms from other causes, with Sinemet relieving the experience of pain but not necessarily proving that Parkinson’s directly caused it.

This is not contemptuous. It is careful and personal. But structurally, it is still gatekeeping. The diagnosis remains the interpretive centre through which the complaint is answered. The therapeutic contradiction is not allowed to force a reclassification of the problem. It is explained within the existing diagnosis. The gate adapts without opening.

That adaptive capacity is one reason diagnostic gatekeeping can endure. A strong frame does not need to deny every contradiction. It only needs to interpret each contradiction as compatible with itself. Severe foot pain? Parkinson’s magnifies pain from other causes. Sinemet response? Parkinson’s medication can relieve pain experience. Atypicality? Young-onset presentations can be complex. Dyskinesia risk? Confirms the caution of slow levodopa escalation. Patient concern? Understandable, sincerely regretted, but not necessarily diagnostic anchoring.

Each individual move may be clinically defensible. The problem is cumulative closure. Nothing is allowed to count strongly enough to reopen the gate.

The 2026 genetic confirmation changes the historical picture without licensing retrospective simplification. The St Vincent’s letter states that genetic testing confirmed two pathogenic variants in the Parkin gene and that Liam’s Parkinsonism and dystonia are due to compound heterozygous mutations in that gene.

This does not prove that 2017 clinicians should have known the answer with certainty. It does, however, show the danger of allowing an idiopathic frame to acquire too much administrative authority before genetic and dystonic uncertainty had been fully governed. What later becomes clear is that the patient was living not merely inside “Parkinson’s disease” as a broad label, but inside a more specific monogenic Parkinsonism with dystonia. The inherited idiopathic gate had been too crude for the reality that later emerged.

That distinction matters. A public scholarly archive should not stage the case as “they said Parkinson’s, but it was not Parkinson’s.” The truth is more interesting and more ethically significant. The later letter says Parkinsonism and dystonia due to Parkin gene mutations. So the problem is not simply the presence or absence of Parkinsonism. The problem is the governing power of an idiopathic frame over a condition whose dystonic, genetic, and foot-centred features required a more cautious architecture.

Diagnosis as gatekeeping also affects patient self-understanding. A diagnosis tells a patient what kind of person he has become medically. It provides expectations: decline, treatment, side effects, prognosis, identity, community, future. If the diagnosis is too final too early, the patient’s own interpretation of his body becomes constrained. He learns to understand his experiences through the sanctioned name. When his feet do not fit, he may experience that mismatch not as evidence of diagnostic incompleteness, but as his own confusion, anxiety, pain sensitivity, or failure to adapt.

This is record-mediated harm at the level of self-knowledge. The record does not stay outside the patient. It enters him. It teaches him what to notice and what to doubt. It tells him which symptoms are central and which are awkward. It tells him which treatments are reasonable and which desires are risky. It tells him whether his protest is structurally meaningful or merely personal distress.

The diagnosis did not simply name the patient; it began to manage the conditions under which he could be understood.

That is why gatekeeping is the right concept. It captures both benefit and harm. The Parkinson’s diagnosis opened access to treatment, LTI certification, specialist follow-up, and a clinical pathway. But it also appears to have narrowed the interpretive field. It made certain forms of uncertainty administratively inconvenient. It allowed later contradictions to be processed as management issues rather than reopening signals. It shaped the complaint pathway. It governed the archive through which the patient’s later challenge would be judged.

A fair essay must acknowledge that clinicians need working frames. Without diagnostic frames, medicine cannot function. Every test, prescription, referral, and explanation requires some organising hypothesis. The critique is not of diagnostic framing as such. It is of unqualified reliance on a frame whose unresolved conditions were still active.

The ethical demand is modest but demanding: when diagnosis governs, the record must preserve the conditions under which it may be revised.

That means a patient-facing and institution-facing record should distinguish clearly between diagnosis as working hypothesis, diagnosis as administrative fact, diagnosis as statutory certification, diagnosis as treatment pathway, and diagnosis as identity-shaping explanation. These are not the same thing. In this case, they appear to have converged too quickly.

Once they converged, the burden shifted. The patient no longer had to challenge only a doctor’s opinion. He had to challenge a system of reliance: discharge summary, LTI form, DAT addendum, medication history, annual letters, complaints classification, and later institutional defence. The gate was not one door. It was many doors aligned behind the same name.

That is the broader lesson for diagnostic-error research and health-data ethics. Harm does not occur only when a diagnosis is wrong. Harm can occur when a diagnosis becomes too powerful before the system has earned that power. It can occur when a provisional name becomes a passport, a filter, a shield, and a jurisdictional boundary all at once.

In this case, the diagnosis opened care. But it also appears to have closed inquiry.

That double movement is the heart of diagnostic governance. A diagnosis should guide the patient through the system. It should not become the system’s reason for refusing to look again.
