Record-Mediated Harm and the Ethics of Data Accuracy

This essay examines how clinical records continue to organise care, complaint handling, identity, and data accuracy after they are written.

A clinical record is not inert.

It keeps working.

It works when a GP reads it. It works when a pharmacist dispenses under a scheme. It works when a consultant inherits a diagnosis. It works when a complaint officer classifies a complaint. It works when a regulator receives a file. It works when the patient tries to understand himself. It works when later clinicians decide whether a symptom is central, peripheral, expected, irrelevant, psychological, or already explained.

That is why data accuracy in medicine cannot be reduced to whether a clinician honestly wrote what they believed at the time. Honesty matters, but it is not enough. A record can be historically sincere and still become presently misleading if it continues to govern after its uncertainty has been stripped of context.

This case is a study in that problem.

The original GP referral transmitted a diagnostically open problem. It described bilateral foot issues, unusual stiff and festinant gait, difficulty initiating movement, increased tone and stiffness, brisk reflexes, possible cogwheeling, worsening dexterity, and functional deterioration threatening employment. Parkinsonism was queried, but not asserted as settled.

From a data-ethics perspective, that referral is accurate not merely because its statements may be true, but because its posture matches its knowledge. It does not pretend to know more than it knows. It preserves uncertainty. It defines the presenting problem without closing the explanatory frame.

The later record changes the data environment. The discharge summary declares “Primary Diagnosis: Parkinsons Disease” while also recording young-onset Parkinsonism, atypical features, genetic possibilities, dystonic-looking gait, unresolved foot and leg symptoms, and uncertainty about whether leg symptoms, corns, callouses, and burning may have been separate or neuropathic.

That kind of record may be clinically understandable. But data accuracy is contextual. A diagnosis in the primary-diagnosis position does not have the same effect as a tentative phrase buried in narrative. A future reader may see the field before the caveat. A system may process the label without the uncertainty. A later complaint may be classified through the authority of the label rather than through the incompleteness surrounding it.

Data accuracy therefore depends on structure, not just content.

If a record contains uncertainty but arranges certainty as the governing element, it may become misleading in use. Not because a clinician lied. Not because the diagnosis was necessarily indefensible. But because the data object, as processed and relied upon, may no longer communicate the true epistemic state of the case.

The Long Term Illness certification intensifies this problem. The LTI form certified that Liam was under care for treatment of Parkinson’s and listed medications under that certified condition. It was stamped by the Neurology Clinic on 09 July 2017.

This is data becoming administrative reality. The label is no longer only in a letter. It becomes part of entitlement, prescribing, pharmacy, and institutional reliance. It may have enabled access to care. That matters. But the ethical cost is that the diagnostic label now travels more easily than the uncertainty that accompanied it.

The record has become portable. The caveats may not be.

The DAT scan addendum then describes the scan as abnormal “as was expected,” with marked findings in both basal ganglia.

Again, the issue is not whether the DAT scan was abnormal. The issue is what the record now teaches later readers. “As expected” positions the scan as confirmatory within an already active frame. The data chain becomes progressively self-reinforcing: referral uncertainty becomes discharge diagnosis; discharge diagnosis becomes certification; certification becomes reliance; scan becomes expected confirmation; later symptoms are interpreted inside the inherited structure.

This is how record-mediated harm works. The harm is not located in a single sentence. It emerges from cumulative reliance.

The longitudinal notes show that the record continued to capture contradictory or complicating material. In 2025, the record says that the central issue on Liam’s mind is foot pain. It preserves his account that his feet have disturbed him since 2005, that the first 150 footsteps are torture, that medications relieve the feet, and that the pain is severe enough to require existential language.

But the presence of this material does not automatically cure the data problem. Later additions can enrich a record without correcting its governing structure. A new note saying “the feet are central to the patient” may coexist with an old diagnosis that still controls interpretation. A medication response may be documented while still being explained within the prior frame. A contradiction may be added to the file without being allowed to qualify the file.

Data accuracy requires more than accumulation.

It requires that new information change the status of old information where appropriate.

That is the key. In medicine, records are longitudinal. They are not static historical artefacts. A statement may be accurate as a contemporaneous belief in 2017, but if later events reveal that it is incomplete, misleading, or insufficiently qualified, continued reliance on it may require annotation, restriction, correction, or contextualisation.

This is especially important when the record concerns diagnostic uncertainty. A diagnosis is not ordinary data. It is organising data. It changes the meaning of other data around it. If the diagnosis is too strong, other facts are weakened. If the diagnosis is incomplete, other facts may be misclassified.

The GDPR derivative document makes this argument explicitly. It frames the issue as continuing inaccuracy and unlawful reliance on administratively foreclosed clinical data. It states that the problem is not diagnostic disagreement, but ongoing data inaccuracy caused by structural incompleteness at the point of finality, compounded by continuing reliance and refusal to qualify or restrict processing.

For the archive, the value of this framing is scholarly rather than adjudicative. It does not require claiming that a regulator has agreed. It allows the case to be analysed as a problem of medical data ethics: when a clinical record continues to govern, what duties arise when its original structure no longer adequately represents uncertainty, contradiction, or later causal clarification?

The 2026 correspondence with the Data Protection Commission shows that the data complaint was under assessment. The DPC acknowledged that issues raised in the complaint were under assessment and later correspondence stated that the complaint had been transferred to the Complaint Assessment and Early Resolution Unit, with further information potentially required.

That procedural status should be treated carefully. Under assessment is not a finding. It does not prove breach. But it confirms that the patient had translated the dispute into a data-protection register: not only “what did doctors think?” but “what personal data continues to be processed, relied upon, and presented as accurate?”

That is the correct ethical expansion.

A patient is not only a body in a clinic. He is also a data subject inside health systems. His diagnosis is processed by systems. His history is transmitted between actors. His complaint is classified through records. His future care depends on old entries. If the record is inaccurate by omission, over-finality, or lack of qualification, the harm is not merely historical. It continues.

The St Vincent’s confirmation sharpens the question. The 2026 letter states that Liam has two pathogenic variants in the Parkin gene and that his Parkinsonism and dystonia are due to compound heterozygous mutations in the Parkin gene.

This new causal statement does not mean all prior records were false in every respect. It does not erase Parkinsonism. It refines and corrects the causal architecture. It moves the case from idiopathic framing toward monogenic Parkinsonism with dystonia. It gives centrality to dystonia in a way the earlier frame did not.

Data accuracy now requires integration.

A record that continues to foreground idiopathic Parkinson’s without adequate qualification after PRKN confirmation risks becoming misleading, even if it was once a reasonable working label. The question becomes: how does the system ensure that the old frame no longer governs unqualified? How are downstream recipients notified? How is the patient’s record amended to reflect monogenic causation and dystonia? How are previous interpretations of foot pain and medication response qualified?

These are not merely technical questions. They are moral questions about whether the patient can finally be accurately represented.

There is a temptation to think of GDPR or data accuracy as bureaucratic compared with the clinical drama of pain, diagnosis, and medication. That would be a mistake. In this case, data accuracy is where the clinical harm becomes durable. The record is the medium through which the original uncertainty travels into future decisions.

If the record remains wrong, incomplete, or misleading, the patient remains trapped in the old case.

This is why rectification matters. Rectification is not about rewriting history to make everyone look wrong. It is about ensuring that a living record no longer misleads living systems. It can preserve historical statements while adding necessary qualification. It can say: this was the working view at the time; this was uncertain; this feature was unresolved; later genetic testing confirmed PRKN-related Parkinsonism and dystonia; prior idiopathic terminology should not be relied upon without qualification.

That kind of amendment is not vengeance. It is accuracy.

It is also clinically useful. A future clinician reading the record needs to know the current causal frame. They need to know that dystonia is not decorative. They need to know that the foot-centred history is not peripheral noise. They need to know that old neuropathic or idiopathic explanations may be incomplete. They need to know how medication response should be interpreted under the updated diagnosis.

Accurate data protects future care.

It also protects patient dignity. To be inaccurately recorded is not merely to be administratively inconvenienced. It is to be misrepresented in the system that speaks for you when you are not in the room. The record travels ahead of the patient. It tells others who he is before he can explain himself. If it carries an incomplete or over-finalised version of him, it shapes the reception of everything he later says.

That is epistemic harm through data.

The patient may say, “the feet are central,” but the record may already have taught the reader that the feet are neuropathy, pain amplification, podiatry, or a complication inside idiopathic Parkinson’s. The patient may say, “Sinemet changed the pain,” but the record may already have taught the reader that Sinemet relieves pain experience without diagnostic significance. The patient may say, “this was genetic dystonia-related Parkinsonism,” but the old frame may still govern if not actively corrected.

A data subject in healthcare is therefore not asking merely for neat paperwork. He is asking for the conditions of being understood.

This is the ethical heart of record-mediated harm.

The record mediates between the body and the institution. If the mediation is distorted, the body must keep reintroducing itself. That is exhausting. It forces the patient to become historian, advocate, analyst, and corrective instrument. He must carry the burden that the record should carry for him.

A good record should reduce that burden. It should not require the patient to keep proving that the old frame is incomplete.

In this case, the archive should argue that data accuracy includes at least four duties.

First, historical fidelity: preserve what clinicians actually thought and did at the time.

Second, contextual qualification: mark what was uncertain, unresolved, or provisional.

Third, longitudinal correction: update the governing frame when later evidence materially changes causal understanding.

Fourth, reliance control: ensure that downstream actors do not continue to process old conclusions as if they remain unqualified.

These duties are compatible. Correcting a record does not require pretending the past was different. It requires preventing the past from misleading the present.

That sentence is essential.

The case is not asking the record to forget 2017. It is asking the record to stop allowing 2017’s provisional frame to dominate after later evidence has changed the epistemic landscape.

This is where GDPR language and medical humanities language meet. Data accuracy is not only compliance. It is narrative justice. It is the correction of the official story so that the patient no longer has to live under an outdated or incomplete account of himself.

The medical record is the authorised biography of the patient within the health system.

If that biography is wrong in structure, the patient lives under misdescription. If it is corrected, not by erasure but by qualification and integration, the patient can finally encounter a system that recognises the shape of his life.

That is why record-mediated harm belongs at the centre of the GitHub Pages archive. It links diagnosis, testimony, governance, and data. It shows that a case can continue to injure after the clinic visit ends because the record continues to act.

A record can open care.

A record can close inquiry.

A record can preserve suffering.

A record can bury uncertainty.

A record can carry an outdated identity into the future.

And when it does, accuracy is not clerical.

It is ethical repair.
