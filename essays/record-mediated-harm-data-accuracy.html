Record-Mediated Harm and the Ethics of Data Accuracy

A record can be historically sincere and still become presently misleading if it continues to govern after its uncertainty has been stripped of context.

Publication note: this essay does not assert that any regulator has made a finding. It treats data accuracy as a scholarly and ethical issue arising from continuing reliance on contested clinical records.

A clinical record is not inert. It keeps working.

It works when a GP reads it. It works when a pharmacist dispenses under a scheme. It works when a consultant inherits a diagnosis. It works when a complaint officer classifies a complaint. It works when a regulator receives a file. It works when the patient tries to understand himself.

That is why data accuracy in medicine cannot be reduced to whether a clinician honestly wrote what they believed at the time. Honesty matters, but it is not enough. A record can be historically sincere and still become presently misleading if it continues to govern after its uncertainty has been stripped of context.

The GP referral was accurate in posture as well as content. It preserved diagnostic openness. It described a foot-centred gait failure, abnormal tone, stiffness, and deteriorating function, without prematurely closing the explanatory frame.

The later record changed the data environment. The discharge summary declared Parkinson's disease as the primary diagnosis while also recording atypicality, possible genetic considerations, dystonic-looking gait, and unresolved foot and leg symptoms. That may have been clinically understandable, but data accuracy is contextual. A primary diagnostic field does not have the same force as a caveat embedded in narrative.

If a record contains uncertainty but arranges certainty as the governing element, it may become misleading in use. Not because a clinician lied, and not because the diagnosis was necessarily indefensible, but because the data object as processed and relied upon may no longer communicate the true epistemic state of the case.

The Long Term Illness certification intensified this. The label became portable. It entered entitlement, prescribing, pharmacy, and institutional reliance. The diagnosis travelled more easily than the uncertainty around it.

Later notes added complicating material: the feet remained central, medication relieved them from a pain perspective, and the patient continued to describe the foot problem as governing his life. But accumulation is not correction. New material can be added to a file without changing the authority of the old frame.

Data accuracy therefore requires more than storage. It requires contextual qualification, longitudinal correction, and control of reliance where old conclusions continue to govern present care.

The GDPR materials in this project should be read in that light. They are not a detour away from care. They are an attempt to address the record through the legal and ethical mechanism available when the record itself continues to organise care.

Article 16 rectification matters because the issue became one of accuracy. Article 18 restriction matters because continued reliance on disputed data can itself become clinically significant. Article 15 access matters because a patient cannot test omissions, teaching interactions, or record gaps without knowing what data exists and who processed it.

The later genetic confirmation does not erase the earlier record. It changes the current accuracy obligation. The record may preserve what was thought at the time, but it must also prevent the earlier idiopathic frame from continuing to govern unqualified after PRKN-related Parkinsonism and dystonia have been confirmed.

Correction does not require pretending the past was different. It requires preventing the past from misleading the present.

That is the ethical meaning of record-mediated harm. The record travels ahead of the patient. It tells others who he is before he can explain himself. If it carries an incomplete or over-finalised version of him, it shapes the reception of everything he later says.

Data accuracy is therefore not clerical. It is the condition under which the patient can be recognised by the system that governs his care.
