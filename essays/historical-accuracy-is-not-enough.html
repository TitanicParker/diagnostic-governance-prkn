Historical Accuracy Is Not Enough

A record can preserve an earlier view and still need later qualification.

Medical records have a historical life and an operational life. Historically, a note records what was seen, believed, or planned at a particular moment. Operationally, the same note later guides care, interpretation, referrals, access, and institutional response.

This distinction is central to the case study. It is possible for an earlier note to be faithful to the view held at the time and still require qualification when later information changes the context in which the note is being used.

Correction does not have to mean erasure. It can mean adding context: what was provisional, what remained unresolved, what later evidence changed, and how future readers should handle older formulations.

The record is therefore not only memory. It is infrastructure. If it continues to organise care and interpretation, it must remain answerable to later knowledge.
