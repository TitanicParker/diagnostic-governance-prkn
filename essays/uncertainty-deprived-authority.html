Uncertainty Deprived of Authority

The problem was not uncertainty; the problem was certainty behaving as though uncertainty had been discharged.

Publication note: this essay does not argue that clinicians should have known the later genetic answer in 2017. It asks a narrower question: where uncertainty was documented, did it retain enough authority to limit diagnostic and administrative finality?

The case is not about uncertainty as such. Medicine is often uncertain. Clinicians work with probabilities, partial evidence, time pressure, risk, and incomplete tests. A serious account of this case should not imply that uncertainty itself is negligent, unethical, or unusual.

The issue is more exact. Uncertainty was recorded, explained, and preserved, but it was not allowed to govern the amount of finality the system created.

The July 2017 record did not present a simple picture. It contained young-onset Parkinsonism, atypical features, possible genetic considerations, dystonic-looking gait, severe foot-centred symptoms, treatment caution, and the future possibility of levodopa. It also contained the foot problem that had brought the patient into care. The record knew enough to remain open.

Yet the same record moved toward closure. Parkinson's disease became the primary diagnostic frame. The patient was discharged within that frame. Long Term Illness certification gave the frame administrative effect. The DAT scan addendum then read the scan as abnormal "as expected," reinforcing a diagnostic structure already in motion.

This is the ethical hinge. Uncertainty can be ornamental, defensive, transitional, or governing. In this case it appears to have been preserved in the first three senses but weakened in the fourth. It was present on the page, but did not prevent the provisional frame from acquiring practical authority.

That matters because finality changes the life of a diagnosis. Once a diagnosis enters discharge summaries, medication schemes, GP records, pharmacy systems, follow-up pathways, occupational understanding, and later complaint handling, it becomes more than a clinical opinion. It becomes a governing reality.

A working diagnosis may be necessary. But where a working diagnosis starts to govern the patient's future, the record must preserve the conditions under which that diagnosis remains revisable. It must say what is unresolved, what would count as contradiction, what has been deferred, and what must happen if the patient's body later speaks against the frame.

The foot-centred problem was the central test of this requirement. The patient entered through the feet. The record repeatedly returned to the feet. Later neurology notes recorded that foot pain remained the issue on his mind and that Parkinson's medication relieved the feet from a pain perspective. The question is not whether every foot symptom was obviously dystonia from the beginning. The question is why the unresolved foot signal did not govern the degree of certainty permitted to the idiopathic frame.

The later St Vincent's confirmation of PRKN-related Parkinsonism and dystonia should not be used as hindsight proof that the answer was knowable in 2017. Its significance is different. It shows that the early uncertainty was not decorative. The atypicality, dystonic language, young onset, foot-centred phenomenology, and genetic question belonged to the structure of the case.

The ethical lesson is therefore not that clinicians must never act until certainty is complete. It is that uncertainty must be allowed to control finality. If inquiry remains incomplete, the record may guide care, but it must not present the guiding frame as more settled than it is.

A record can be sophisticated and still foreclosing. It can mention doubt while acting with certainty. It can preserve complexity while creating reliance. It can show that uncertainty was known and still fail because that uncertainty was not obeyed.

That is what this case exposes. The system did not need to deny uncertainty. It only needed to place uncertainty beneath acts of reliance that uncertainty could no longer control.
