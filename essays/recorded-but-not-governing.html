Recorded but Not Governing

This essay examines how patient testimony can be carefully preserved in the record while remaining subordinate to the governing diagnostic frame.

There is a weak version of this case and a strong version.

The weak version says the patient was not listened to.

The documents do not fully support that. In fact, they complicate it. They show a patient repeatedly heard, quoted, paraphrased, examined, counselled, and copied into correspondence. They show clinicians taking time, recording phrases, describing bodily experience, and sometimes preserving the patient’s words with striking fidelity. A crude claim of non-listening would flatten the case and make it easier to dismiss.

The stronger claim is more uncomfortable.

The patient was listened to, but his testimony was not allowed to govern.

That is the epistemic injustice at the centre of the record. Not erasure, but subordination. Not silence, but controlled admissibility. Not absence from the record, but presence without authority.

The first scene is the GP referral. Before the hospital frame existed, the patient’s condition entered medicine through the feet. The referral described an unusual festinant and stiff gait, bilateral foot issues, worsening movement initiation, increased tone, brisk reflexes, possible cogwheeling, and work-threatening deterioration. Parkinsonism was queried, but not fixed. The GP did not present a settled diagnosis. He transmitted a problem requiring explanation: the patient could not move properly, and the feet were central to how that failure appeared.

That is important because epistemic injustice often begins at the point where a patient’s account is translated into professional language. Translation is necessary in medicine. Symptoms must become signs, histories, referrals, differentials, plans. But translation is never neutral. Something is always carried forward, and something is left behind. In this case, the referral transmitted the feet as an initiating signal. The later diagnostic frame increasingly treated the feet as secondary, external, peripheral, or interpretively troublesome.

The patient’s testimony was not rejected outright. It was absorbed. That is the more serious mechanism.

The 2017 record preserved many of Liam’s own formulations: tension in the body, difficulty synchronising arms and legs, deteriorating writing, diminished dexterity, leg tension, foot problems, fallen arches, corns and calluses, and the sense that previous symptoms had matured into the current state. The record did not lack detail. It contained a great deal of the patient. But the presence of patient language inside a medical document does not mean the patient’s interpretation has been granted epistemic standing.

A record can quote a person while using the quotation to support a different structure.

That is what happens when testimony becomes descriptive evidence rather than governing evidence. Descriptive evidence is allowed to decorate the case. Governing evidence changes what the case is.

The later notes make this distinction painfully clear. In a 2025 neurology note, “the one thing that is on Liam’s mind is foot pain.” The letter then records the foot account in language no abstract diagnostic label can exhaust: “my feet disturb me”; he hobbles out of bed; the feet are too sore to tie runners; each morning feels like “murder in the feet”; the first 150 footsteps are torture; he feels he is festinating from the pain; and saying “I won’t live with it” gives words to the situation.

That is not a failure to hear. It is, in one sense, remarkable hearing. The patient’s phenomenology is preserved. His words are not sanitised into “mild discomfort.” They remain vivid, severe, embodied.

But what happens next is the ethical issue. The note acknowledges that once Parkinson’s medications hit his system, “the feet feel better from a pain perspective.” It then says Sinemet “should not do anything for pain” but may relieve the experience of pain, using the analogy of old ankle or leg fractures. It continues: pain is “not really a direct part of Parkinson’s,” though painful dystonia can occur, and yet increasing Sinemet would probably provide some further relief, limited by dyskinesia risk.

This is the structure of recorded-but-not-governing testimony in its purest form. The patient reports the feet. The record preserves the report. The medication response is acknowledged. The possibility of dystonia is named. But the interpretive centre remains elsewhere. The pain is recognised, then explained around. It is treated as something Parkinson’s may magnify, something Lyrica may address, something Sinemet may modulate, something dyskinesia risk may limit — but not as the signal that should reorganise the diagnostic frame.

The testimony is present, but the frame survives.

This matters because testimonial injustice is often imagined as disbelief. A patient says something; the clinician dismisses it. That form exists, of course. But this case shows a more sophisticated and institutionally durable form. The patient says something; the clinician records it; the record becomes proof that the patient was heard; yet the recorded material does not alter the hierarchy of explanation.

This is harder to challenge because the institution can point to the very richness of the record as evidence of attentiveness. The patient’s words are there. His pain is there. His questions are there. His unease is there. His account of medication response is there. But the ethical question is not whether the record contains him. The question is what power his presence has.

A person can be fully documented and still epistemically overruled.

The 2023 clinic material shows the same pattern. Liam describes an episode after “Energetic Healing,” the resurgence of foot pain, difficulty walking, and the feeling that the “attention to walking” itself is painful. The note records cramping in the left lower limb and explicitly raises the question of Parkinsonian dystonia, likely OFF dystonia. It also records the persistence of pain around the edge of the feet, the inability to do a fourth walk at night, the outside-left-foot discomfort, and the patient’s belief that the walk psychologically consumes him.

Again, the record is not thin. It is not indifferent in the obvious sense. It contains nuance. It even resists a simple psychologising interpretation. The note states that this does not look like a “simply psychological process” or functional neurological disorder. It acknowledges that the problem may involve the lack of levodopa in the evenings. It records Liam’s own formulation that he takes Stalevo for “Pep in the Step” and “for pain.” It recognises a “misalignment” between clinician and patient: the clinician’s firm belief that everything must be explainable by Parkinson’s, and Liam’s “reasonable and justifiable concerns” that much relates to the feet as a separate problem.

That passage is extraordinarily important. It shows that the disagreement was not hidden. It was not absent. It was not invisible to the treating clinician. The record itself names the misalignment.

But even there, the misalignment does not govern. It is acknowledged as a relational and interpretive fact, then folded back into additional testing for rare overlap entities such as Fabry disease, continued Parkinson’s framing, and medication caution. The patient’s concern is treated as reasonable. It is not treated as architecturally decisive.

That is the ethical texture of the case: respect without redistribution of authority.

The patient is credited as intelligent, articulate, observant, and sincere. His phrases are treated as worth preserving. His body is taken seriously enough to be described in detail. But the diagnostic frame retains the power to decide what those reports mean. When the patient says the feet are central, the frame can answer: the feet are affected by Parkinson’s, magnified by Parkinson’s, complicated by neuropathic possibility, influenced by Lyrica, modulated by Sinemet, worsened by OFF periods, shaped by psychology, constrained by dyskinesia risk. Each of these may be clinically plausible in isolation. The ethical problem is cumulative. Together, they allow the original diagnostic architecture to remain intact no matter how often the feet return.

This is how record-mediated epistemic injustice differs from ordinary disagreement. In ordinary disagreement, two interpretations compete. In record-mediated injustice, one interpretation controls the archive in which the disagreement is later judged.

The patient’s testimony enters that archive under conditions set by the diagnostic frame. Later readers encounter his words already surrounded by interpretive scaffolding. He is not simply “Liam saying his feet are the problem.” He is “Liam, a patient with Parkinson’s, reporting foot pain, neuropathic possibilities, dyskinesia concerns, psychological burden, alcohol history, Lyrica changes, and Sinemet response.” The record does not only preserve facts. It arranges credibility.

Arrangement is power.

This is why the case belongs in the literature of epistemic injustice. The injustice is not that the patient had no voice. It is that voice and authority came apart. Liam could describe his body, but the diagnostic frame decided what kind of description it was. He could say the feet were central, but the record could treat them as peripheral, separate, neuropathic, dermatological, psychological, OFF-related, or pain-amplified. He could report medication response, but the response could be absorbed into the existing Parkinson’s explanation. He could articulate unease, but unease could be recorded as part of the clinical encounter rather than as a reason to reopen the case.

In this structure, the patient is not outside the record. He is inside it, but not sovereign over the meaning of his own recurring signal.

The distinction is morally decisive.

A system that simply ignores the patient leaves an obvious wound. A system that documents the patient while subordinating his testimony creates a cleaner surface. It can later say: we heard him; we quoted him; we counselled him; we copied him; we explored his concerns; we spent time. All of that may be true. But none of it answers the governing question: did the patient’s testimony have the power to change the structure?

In this case, the answer appears to be no.

The feet remained narratable but not governing. Dystonia remained mentionable but not organising. Medication response remained acknowledged but not transformative. Diagnostic unease remained recordable but not jurisdictional. The patient’s account became part of the clinical literature of his case, while the case itself continued to be governed by a frame that could metabolise every contradiction as variation within itself.

That is what makes the phrase “recorded but not governing” so useful. It avoids theatrical overstatement. It does not accuse clinicians of refusing to listen. It instead names the documentary mechanism by which listening can occur without epistemic correction.

The patient was heard in the grammar of the record, but not in the governance of the diagnosis.

There is a further injury here. When a patient’s testimony is recorded without being allowed to govern, the record can later be used against the claim that he was marginalised. The abundance of documentation becomes defensive. The institution can point to the very passages that prove the patient’s body was speaking and say: look, we knew; look, we wrote it down; look, we considered it. But the ethical question is not whether the body was transcribed. It is whether the transcription altered the decision-making hierarchy.

In that sense, the record becomes both archive and containment device. It stores the protest while neutralising its force. It preserves the patient’s words while ensuring that those words remain subordinate to professional interpretation. It keeps the body visible, but not authoritative.

The final irony is that the patient’s testimony becomes more legible after later confirmation. The 2026 St Vincent’s genetic result does not prove that PRKN/PARK2 was knowable with certainty in 2017. It should not be used that way. But it does illuminate why the patient’s repeated insistence on the feet, dystonia-like features, medication response, and misfit with the idiopathic frame mattered. It shows that the patient’s account was not merely emotional resistance to diagnosis. It was an embodied signal pressing against a classification that did not fully fit.

This does not mean the patient was always right in every interpretation. Patients can be wrong. Clinicians can reasonably resist patient hypotheses. Medicine cannot be reorganised around every subjective formulation. But this case is not built on one isolated complaint. It is built on recurrence: the same bodily signal, the same foot-centred disturbance, the same response pattern, the same unease, the same mismatch, appearing across years and being documented across years.

At some point, recurrence itself becomes epistemic evidence.

The harm, then, is not only pain. It is not only diagnostic delay. It is not only administrative foreclosure. It is the experience of having one’s body speak repeatedly in a language the record can quote but not obey.

That is a precise form of injustice. It is quieter than outright dismissal and therefore more durable. It leaves fewer obvious fingerprints because the patient’s words remain on the page. But the page has its own hierarchy. In this case, the hierarchy allowed the testimony to be preserved as detail while the diagnostic frame retained command.

The record did not silence the patient.

It converted his speech into material that could be stored without being obeyed.
