The Medical Record as Moral Instrument

A medical record is often treated as a container.

It holds facts. It stores observations. It preserves decisions. It allows one clinician to understand what another clinician saw, thought, and did. It is practical, administrative, evidential, and clinical. It is the machinery by which medicine remembers itself.

But a record does more than remember. It organises what memory will mean.

That is why the medical record is a moral instrument. It does not only describe the patient. It distributes authority over the patient. It decides which facts appear central, which appear secondary, which are carried forward, which are left behind, which doubts remain active, and which conclusions become durable enough to shape future care.

In this case, the record did not merely document a diagnostic process. It became one of the instruments by which the diagnostic process acquired power.

The first record in the chain, the GP referral, performs a clean and ethically important function. It brings Liam into hospital through a foot-centred gait failure. It records bilateral foot issues, an unusual festinant and stiff gait, difficulty initiating movements, increased tone, brisk reflexes, possible cogwheeling, worsening dexterity, and the risk that he may lose his job if not assessed quickly. Parkinsonism is queried, not fixed.

This record preserves openness. It does not pretend to know too much. It names the problem and transfers responsibility. It says, in effect: this man’s body is failing in a way that threatens his work, and the relationship between his feet, gait, tone, and neurological signs must be explained.

That is a morally good record. Not because it is perfect, but because it keeps the problem alive.

The later discharge record has a different moral function. It is more detailed, more sophisticated, and more consequential. It declares Parkinson’s disease as the primary diagnosis while also preserving extensive uncertainty: young-onset Parkinsonism, atypical features, differential possibilities, possible genetic disease, dystonic-looking gait, unresolved foot and leg symptoms, medication caution, and future treatment possibilities.

The document therefore does two things at once. It remembers uncertainty, and it organises finality.

That dual function is the heart of the case. The record contains enough complexity to show that the diagnosis was not simple. But it also places Parkinson’s disease in the primary-diagnosis position, links the patient to Parkinson’s pathways, and allows downstream reliance to begin. The problem is not that the record contains no doubt. The problem is that doubt and authority are unevenly arranged.

A medical record is not only a list of statements. It is a hierarchy.

Where a statement appears matters. A diagnosis in the primary-diagnosis field has a different force from a doubt embedded in a paragraph. A medication plan has a different force from a patient’s unresolved protest. A statutory certification has a different force from a conditional sentence saying the leg symptoms “may have been” separate. A scan described as abnormal “as expected” has a different force from a scan treated as a genuine decision point.

The moral force of the record lies in these arrangements.

The Long Term Illness certification shows this with particular clarity. The form certifies that Liam is under care for treatment of Parkinson’s and lists medications under that condition. It is stamped by the Neurology Clinic on 09 July 2017.

That form is not merely a reflection of clinical thinking. It does something. It gives the diagnosis administrative mobility. It enters the label into systems of entitlement, pharmacy, prescribing, and later reliance. It allows a clinical hypothesis to become a durable administrative fact.

This may have been done for patient benefit. Access to medication matters. Certification can be compassionate and practically necessary. But the moral issue remains: once a diagnosis is certified, it becomes harder for later uncertainty to dislodge it. The record has now created an institutional reality.

That reality then shapes later interpretation.

The DAT scan addendum says the DAT scan was abnormal “as was expected,” with marked findings in both basal ganglia. It states that the report says Parkinson’s Disease or Lewy Body disease, but “this is obviously not LBD.”

Again, this is not a crude error. The DAT scan may well have supported a degenerative parkinsonian syndrome. But the record’s language matters. “As expected” locates the scan inside an existing interpretive structure. It does not present the scan as the point at which the diagnosis becomes entitled to govern. It presents the scan as confirmation of what the record had already begun to treat as true.

The record did not merely remember the case; it taught the system how to remember it.

That teaching effect persists. Later clinicians and administrators do not encounter Liam’s body directly in 2017. They encounter the recorded version of that body. They inherit the diagnosis, the certification, the medication logic, the scan addendum, the correspondence, the annual clinic letters, and the interpretive vocabulary already attached to him. They meet a patient through a record that has already arranged his credibility.

This is why health-data ethics belongs at the centre of the archive. Data accuracy is not just about whether a single statement is factually correct. A record can be individually defensible in many of its parts and still misleading in its structure. It can contain the right details while giving them the wrong authority. It can preserve uncertainty while allowing certainty to dominate. It can include the patient’s testimony while denying that testimony governing power.

The record’s morality lies not only in content, but in use.

The later notes repeatedly preserve the patient’s account of the feet. In 2025, the record says that “the one thing” on Liam’s mind is foot pain. It quotes him describing his feet as disturbing him since 2005, morning footsteps as torture, and the pain as severe enough that saying “I won’t live with it” gives words to the situation. It also records that Parkinson’s medications relieve the feet from a pain perspective.

These are morally important acts of recording. They prevent the body from disappearing. They preserve the patient’s own language. They create an archive of suffering that no later reader can honestly say was absent.

Yet the record’s deeper moral question remains: what did those recorded facts do?

Did they reopen the governing frame? Did they alter the diagnostic hierarchy? Did they make the feet central again? Did they trigger specialist reassessment? Did they change the meaning of earlier uncertainty? Or did they remain, despite their vividness, inside the inherited structure?

A record can honour a patient’s words and still contain them.

That is the uncomfortable lesson. Documentation is not the same as justice. Recording is not the same as correction. A phrase can be preserved and neutralised in the same act. The record can say, “this is what the patient says,” while the system continues to act as though the patient’s words do not change the case.

This is not necessarily because any individual intends containment. It is because the medical record has form. It has fields, conventions, inherited labels, diagnostic headings, medication lists, referral pathways, and professional assumptions. Once a record has acquired a centre, new information tends to orbit that centre unless something deliberately reorders it.

In this case, Parkinson’s disease became that centre.

The feet orbited. Pain orbited. Dystonia orbited. Genetic possibility orbited. Patient protest orbited. Medication response orbited. Later complaint orbited. The centre adapted, explained, and absorbed.

That is why the medical record must be understood as moral architecture. It builds a world in which some facts carry load and others do not.

The March 2025 complaint correspondence shows what happens when the patient tries to challenge that architecture. Liam’s formal complaint raised diagnostic anchoring, failure to reassess, prolonged preventable pain, therapeutic contradiction, and the need for independent review and disclosure. It did not claim malice. It framed the issue as systemic and asked how the outcome was possible.

The closure response classified the complaint as relating solely to clinical judgment and forwarded it to the consultant neurologist for response.

That procedural act is also part of the record’s morality. Once the complaint is classified as clinical judgment, the existing clinical record becomes the main interpretive surface. The patient is no longer contesting only what happened in 2017. He is contesting a record system that has already defined what kind of issue his challenge is allowed to be.

The same thing happens in data-governance terms. The GDPR derivative document frames the issue as continuing inaccuracy and unlawful reliance on administratively foreclosed clinical data, arguing that the problem is not diagnostic disagreement but ongoing processing of inaccurate or misleading data caused by structural incompleteness at the point of finality.

Whether a regulator accepts that framing is a separate question. But analytically, it identifies something crucial: records can continue to harm when their authority exceeds their accuracy, completeness, or contextual qualification. A medical record is not finished when it is written. It continues to operate every time it is relied upon.

This is especially true where a record has patient-facing force. A patient-facing record does not merely inform the patient. It participates in forming the patient’s self-understanding. It tells him what has happened to him, what his symptoms mean, what risks matter, what future he faces, and what kinds of disagreement will be intelligible.

The July 2017 record did this. It gave Liam an explanatory world: young-onset Parkinson’s, caution with levodopa, possible future treatments, genetic considerations, occupational implications, Parkinson’s supports, LTI certification. It did not merely say “you may have this condition.” It structured his life around that condition.

Again, this may have been sincere and medically plausible. The ethical issue is not whether explanation should have been withheld. The ethical issue is whether the explanation sufficiently preserved the active uncertainty and unresolved foot-centred problem that should have constrained how much authority the frame acquired.

The medical record as moral instrument must therefore be judged by four questions.

First, does it preserve the patient’s presenting problem in its original force, or does it translate it into a form that loses the initiating signal?

Second, does it distinguish clearly between working hypothesis and final diagnosis?

Third, does it identify what would require revision of the frame?

Fourth, does it allow the patient’s testimony to become governing evidence when it repeatedly returns to the same unresolved point?

In this case, those questions expose the failure mode. The record preserved much, but not always in the right order. It displayed uncertainty, but did not consistently grant uncertainty authority. It recorded the feet, but did not keep them central. It included the patient, but did not fully disclose the decision architecture. It allowed the diagnosis to travel farther than the unresolved evidence should safely have permitted.

That is record-mediated harm.

Record-mediated harm does not require fabrication. It does not require malice. It does not require a villain. It can arise when a record’s structure causes later actors to see too much through one frame. It can arise when administrative systems convert clinical uncertainty into practical certainty. It can arise when a patient’s own testimony is preserved as narrative but denied the power to reorder care.

The 2026 genetic confirmation sharpens, but does not create, the issue. The St Vincent’s letter states that Liam has two pathogenic variants in the Parkin gene and that his Parkinsonism and dystonia are due to compound heterozygous Parkin mutations.

That later clarity does not mean the 2017 record should have contained the answer. It means the earlier record needed to remain more open to the kind of answer that eventually arrived. The record needed to protect uncertainty not as embarrassment, but as truth.

This is the final moral point. The medical record is not merely accountable to what clinicians believe at the time. It is accountable to the future patient who must live inside the consequences of that belief. It must leave room for correction. It must mark uncertainty where uncertainty matters. It must prevent administrative convenience from becoming epistemic certainty. It must not let a provisional frame become so authoritative that later truth appears disruptive rather than clarifying.

A record can heal by making suffering legible.

It can harm by making one interpretation too powerful.

In this case, the record did both. It preserved Liam’s suffering in unusual detail. It also appears to have organised that suffering under a frame that became harder and harder to reopen.

That is why the medical record must be treated not as a neutral archive, but as a moral instrument.

It remembers.

It classifies.

It authorises.

It governs.

And when it governs prematurely, it can injure long after the original consultation has ended.
