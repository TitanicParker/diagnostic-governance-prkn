The Feet as the Originating Signal

This essay examines the feet as the originating bodily signal in the case and the ethical consequences of repeatedly displacing that signal within the diagnostic record.

The feet are not a subplot.

They are the beginning of the case.

Before the diagnostic vocabulary hardens, before Parkinson’s disease becomes the dominant name, before the later language of idiopathic versus genetic, before complaint, governance, data accuracy, and institutional response, there is a simpler fact: Liam entered the medical system through a problem of walking, and that problem was experienced, described, and referred through the feet.

The GP referral of 29 June 2017 is crucial because it precedes specialist translation. It does not yet carry the later institutional weight of a settled neurological frame. It describes a man with an unusual festinant and stiff gait, bilateral foot issues, difficulty initiating movement, increased tone, brisk reflexes, possible cogwheeling, no overt resting tremor, worsening dexterity, and work-threatening functional deterioration. The GP records that Liam had been attending with bilateral foot issues and had attributed his gait to callouses and corns; the GP then escalates the matter because the presentation has become neurologically concerning.

That referral is diagnostically disciplined. It does not declare Parkinson’s disease. It asks for neurological opinion. It queries “parkinsonian or other underlying diagnosis” because the signs are now serious and deteriorating.

The ethical significance lies in that ordering. The feet do not appear after diagnosis as an inconvenient complication. They are already there at entry. They are part of why the patient is referred, why urgency exists, why work is threatened, and why neurology is asked to intervene. The feet are not merely painful extremities attached to a neurological patient. They are the pathway by which the case enters specialist medicine.

This matters because diagnostic governance begins with problem definition. A system can only answer the question it agrees to ask. The GP referral asks, in effect: why can this man no longer walk properly, and what is the relation between his foot-centred disturbance and his abnormal neurological signs? Once the question is translated into a more abstract problem — increased tone, rigidity, bradykinesia, young-onset Parkinsonism — the feet risk losing their governing status. They remain in the story, but they no longer define the problem.

That is the first displacement.

The July 2017 clinical summary contains a striking amount of foot material. It records corns, calluses, fallen arches, foot treatment since 2009 or 2010, and the patient’s own developing distinction between foot pain and leg tension. It notes pes planus and hammertoes. It describes a gait that is “strikingly abnormal,” with a footdrop-like appearance suggestive of dystonic posture, while explicitly saying “NOT FOOT DROP.” It preserves a complex picture: foot pathology, abnormal gait, dystonic appearance, young-onset Parkinsonism, atypical features, and uncertainty.

Yet the same document conditionally separates the leg and foot symptoms from the main diagnostic structure. It states that the leg symptoms, including corns, callouses, and burning, “may have been completely separate” or perhaps secondary peripheral neuropathy, possibly related in some respect to original alcohol intake; it adds that this is “not clear.”

Those words deserve careful handling. They are not proof of indifference. They show uncertainty. They show an attempt to account for multiple possibilities. A clinician facing a complex presentation may reasonably wonder whether dermatological, orthopaedic, neuropathic, and neurological processes are co-existing. The ethical issue is not that separation was considered. The ethical issue is that separation remained conditional at the same time that diagnostic finality began to operate elsewhere.

The grammar is revealing. Parkinson’s becomes the organising diagnosis. The feet “may have been” separate. The diagnostic frame is declarative; the originating signal is modal. The centre is asserted; the beginning is qualified.

That is the second displacement.

The feet are then partly externalised through podiatry. The plan asks for outpatient chiropody or podiatry referral, noting that an SHO had tried to do this in hospital. Again, podiatry was not irrational. The record contained corns, calluses, pes planus, hammertoes, and long-standing foot treatment. It would have been clinically reasonable to seek foot care. But ethically and diagnostically, podiatry could not answer the whole question unless the neurological status of the foot signal had first been resolved.

A podiatry referral can be helpful. It can remove a peripheral contributor. It can treat surface pathology. It can improve comfort. But it cannot, by itself, explain why a young man has rigidity, bradykinesia, dystonic gait appearance, toe clawing, medication-responsive movement disturbance, and a foot-centred walking failure. When podiatry becomes the container for the feet, the initiating signal is moved out of the neurological problem rather than integrated into it.

That is not necessarily a conscious act. It is a structural one. The body is divided into departments. The feet become feet. The gait becomes neurology. The pain becomes neuropathy or podiatry or later Lyrica. But the lived phenomenon does not divide itself so cleanly. For Liam, the problem was walking, pain, posture, toe curling, effort, surface contact, initiation, and movement as one embodied field.

The record repeatedly returns to this fact.

In 2023, the foot-centred signal resurfaces with force. Liam describes worsening foot pain after an episode of energetic healing, difficulty walking, attention to walking as painful, cramping in the left lower limb, and the question is raised in the note of Parkinsonian dystonia, whether ON or OFF, “clearly more likely an OFF dystonia.” The note records a “corona of pain around the edge of the feet,” inability to do a fourth walk at night, bare feet in the morning, shoes hurting the feet, and major discomfort on the outside of the left foot.

That is not a random symptom drifting through the record. It is the old signal returning in more specific language: feet, edge of feet, outside-left-foot discomfort, walking failure, toe and leg cramping, OFF phenomena, dystonia question, medication timing, daily function.

The record also shows that Liam tries to conceptualise the problem. He says the pain is “not pain” but the “pain of the attention it takes to walk.” He describes the walk as psychologically consuming him. He describes the fourth walk as a psychological, social, and physical barrier. The note itself resists reducing this to a purely psychological process or functional neurological disorder. It recognises that psychological aspects can worsen Parkinsonian symptoms, but does not treat the whole problem as “simply psychological.”

This is diagnostically and ethically important. The patient is not merely saying “my feet hurt.” He is trying to describe a disorder of walking as lived from the feet upward: the demand of attention, the protective stance, short steps, lack of movement, cramping, outside-foot discomfort, and the collapse of ease. These are not ordinary pain descriptors. They are movement descriptors experienced as foot suffering.

The feet are the place where the disorder becomes undeniable.

By 2025, the record is even more explicit. “The one thing that is on Liam’s mind is foot pain.” The note then gives one of the clearest phenomenological passages in the corpus: since 2005, “my feet disturb me”; he hobbles out of bed; he takes medications but the feet are too sore to tie runners; each morning feels like “murder in the feet”; the first 150 footsteps are torture; he feels he is festinating from the pain; and putting the sentence “I won’t live with it” on the page gives words to the situation.

That passage should anchor the essay because it shows recurrence across two decades. The feet are not an incidental late complaint. They are dated by the patient back to 2005. They are linked to morning function, walking initiation, footwear, pain, medication timing, and endurance. They are not just anatomical; they are existential. They determine whether the day can begin.

The same 2025 note records that once Parkinson’s medications hit his system, Liam experiences relief in the feet. It also says Sinemet “should not do anything for pain,” while acknowledging that it may relieve the experience of pain. It then states that pain is not really a direct part of Parkinson’s, though painful dystonia can occur, and that the described pain is not a direct Parkinson’s phenomenology. Yet the note also says there is little doubt that increasing Sinemet would probably give further relief, though limited by dyskinesias.

This is the foot signal trapped in interpretive limbo. It is too responsive to medication to be ignored. It is too painful and persistent to be incidental. It is too atypical to sit comfortably inside ordinary idiopathic Parkinson’s pain explanation. It is too entangled with dystonia and OFF symptoms to be purely podiatric. Yet every explanatory route seems to stop short of allowing the feet to become the organising problem.

The feet remain present, but displaced.

This is why the ethical argument must be disciplined. It should not claim that the feet were obviously and exclusively caused by PRKN-related dystonia from the outset. That would be hindsight. It should not deny that corns, calluses, pes planus, hammertoes, alcohol history, neuropathy, or mechanical pain were legitimate considerations. The corpus itself contains reasons why clinicians would think about those things. The stronger argument is that because those considerations never satisfactorily resolved the foot-centred signal, they should not have been allowed to demote it permanently.

A bodily signal can be complex without being disposable.

The March 2025 response makes this dynamic especially visible. Dr Monaghan apologises for Liam’s experience and acknowledges that he had not understood this was how Liam felt. He accepts that Sinemet helps the pain experience, while explaining this through Parkinson’s magnification of pain from other causes and the risks of escalating Sinemet in a younger person. He states that the care had not been “completely anchored” to alcohol-related peripheral neuropathy and points to attempts to find other causes, including Fabry testing.

That response is important because it is not crude denial. It accepts pain. It accepts suffering. It accepts medication response. It explains caution. It gives a clinically coherent account of why dopaminergic escalation was handled slowly. But structurally, it again keeps the feet inside the prior frame. The feet become pain that Parkinson’s may magnify, neuropathy that may or may not exist, symptoms that Sinemet may relieve, but not the original organising clue that should force reconsideration of the frame itself.

The contradiction is converted into continuity.

The ethical significance of the feet, then, is not simply that they hurt. It is that they preserve sequence. They remember the case before the case had a name. They carry the entry condition forward through years of diagnostic, therapeutic, and administrative elaboration. When later records say “the one thing” on Liam’s mind is foot pain, they are not introducing a new theme. They are returning to the beginning.

This is why the feet matter for clinical ethics. They resist abstraction. Diagnostic labels can become elegant. “Young-onset Parkinsonism,” “idiopathic Parkinson’s disease,” “peripheral neuropathic symptomatology,” “non-motor fluctuation,” “OFF dystonia,” “dyskinesia,” “pain amplification” — each phrase has clinical value. But each also risks smoothing the lived body into a manageable category. The feet interrupt that smoothing. They insist that the case is not only about classification, but about the particular place where the patient’s world breaks down.

The first 150 footsteps are torture.

That sentence does more analytic work than pages of diagnostic vocabulary. It tells us that the disorder is not merely inside the brain, not merely inside the record, not merely inside a reimbursement category, and not merely inside a dispute about naming. It is in the passage from bed to shoes, from shoes to door, from door to the first part of the day. It is in the body’s negotiation with ground.

A diagnostic system that cannot keep that fact central has not merely made a technical mistake. It has lost contact with the patient’s governing reality.

There is also an important philosophical dimension here. Medicine often distinguishes symptoms by origin: central versus peripheral, neurological versus mechanical, motor versus sensory, disease versus complication. Those distinctions are necessary. But the patient’s body presents experience before it presents taxonomy. The feet may be the site where central motor disorder, dystonic posture, pain, cutaneous pressure, medication state, anxiety, learned protection, and mechanical foot changes all converge. The fact that the feet are diagnostically complex is precisely why they should have remained central.

Complexity should increase attention, not justify displacement.

This case therefore offers a general lesson for diagnostic governance: the first bodily signal must remain accountable across the life of the record. If later clinicians believe it has been superseded, the record should show how. If it has been explained, the record should say by what mechanism. If it has been falsified, the record should show the test. If it is being externalised to another specialty, the record should make clear what neurological questions remain. If it is being treated as separate, the separation should not remain merely conditional while the main diagnosis becomes administratively durable.

The feet were never absent from the record; they were present in the wrong grammatical position.

They appeared as history, complication, podiatry issue, neuropathic possibility, pain problem, medication problem, alcohol-context problem, psychological burden, and OFF-related difficulty. They were allowed many secondary identities. What they struggled to recover was primacy.

That is the moral injury. Not that every answer was available. Not that every clinician should have known the genetic endpoint. Not that caution was indefensible. But that the body’s first and most persistent signal was repeatedly made subordinate to a frame that never fully explained it.

A serious archive must therefore keep returning to the feet, not for rhetorical effect, but for methodological accuracy. The feet are the thread that prevents the case from becoming merely abstract. They connect referral to discharge, discharge to follow-up, follow-up to complaint, complaint to rebuttal, and later genetic confirmation to the original embodied problem.

They are the originating signal.

And in a case about diagnostic governance, the originating signal is not background. It is the question the system accepted responsibility to answer.
