Uncertainty Deprived of Authority

This essay argues that the ethical failure was not the presence of uncertainty, but the loss of uncertainty's authority over diagnostic finality.

The case is not about uncertainty.

That must be said at the outset because uncertainty is not a scandal in medicine. It is one of medicine’s ordinary working conditions. Doctors diagnose under incomplete information. They use probabilities. They treat before every possible alternative has been eliminated. They protect patients from unnecessary delay. They balance risk against benefit. They choose paths under conditions that rarely offer total clarity.

A serious account of this case must therefore avoid the cheap argument that because uncertainty existed, clinicians should have done nothing, decided nothing, or waited for perfect knowledge. That would be medically naïve and ethically unfair.

The failure is different.

The failure is that uncertainty was recorded, but not allowed to govern.

The July 2017 record did not present a simple, unqualified picture. It preserved complexity. It recorded young-onset Parkinsonism, possible idiopathic Parkinson’s disease, possible autosomal recessive Parkinson’s disease, atypical features, dystonic gait appearance, minimal tremor, genetic questions, unresolved foot and leg symptoms, pending investigations, medication caution, and the possibility that future regret might attach to delaying levodopa.

That is not a record ignorant of uncertainty. It is a record saturated with uncertainty.

The ethical question is what the record did with that uncertainty.

Uncertainty can have different statuses. It can be ornamental, appearing as a caveat without affecting the action taken. It can be defensive, protecting a clinician from overstatement. It can be transitional, marking what remains to be clarified. Or it can be governing, determining how much finality the system is entitled to create.

In this case, uncertainty appears to have been preserved in the first three senses but weakened in the fourth. It was written down. It was explained. It was contextualised. It was even shared with the patient. But when the system moved to discharge, certification, diagnostic labelling, and downstream reliance, uncertainty did not prevent the frame from hardening.

That is the structural problem.

The discharge summary declares “Primary Diagnosis: Parkinsons Disease.” It also contains uncertainty about the exact nature of the Parkinsonian syndrome, the relationship between foot symptoms and the diagnosis, the possible genetic dimension, and future treatment sequencing.

This coexistence is not automatically improper. A discharge summary may contain both a working diagnosis and unresolved questions. But the more consequential the downstream reliance, the more precise the record must be about the provisional status of what is being relied upon. If the diagnosis is operating as a working hypothesis, the record must protect its revisability. If it is operating as a settled administrative fact, the record must have earned that settlement.

The case turns on that distinction.

The Long Term Illness certification shows why. The LTI form certified that Liam was under care for treatment of Parkinson’s and listed medications under that certified condition. It was stamped by the Neurology Clinic on 09 July 2017.

Again, one can understand why this happened. Certification may have been intended to support access to medication. It may have been practically helpful. It may have reflected the treating team’s best working view. But certification is not just compassion. It is also finality. It gives the diagnosis administrative life. It allows the label to travel through systems that are not designed to preserve fine-grained diagnostic uncertainty.

Once that occurs, uncertainty must become more than a paragraph. It must become a constraint.

The record needed to say, in effect: Parkinsonism is strongly suspected and is being used for care planning and access, but the exact causal frame remains unresolved; the foot-centred initiating problem has not yet been fully explained; genetic and atypical features remain live; levodopa is being deferred for reasons of long-term management but may also have diagnostic significance; the diagnosis must remain reviewable if the foot signal, treatment response, or later investigations contradict the working model.

That kind of language would not require clinicians to know the future. It would simply preserve uncertainty as operational. It would prevent the diagnosis from becoming more settled administratively than it was epistemically.

The DAT scan addendum moved in the opposite direction. It described the abnormal DAT scan as “as expected,” with marked findings in both basal ganglia, and immediately narrowed the scan report’s possible interpretation by excluding Lewy Body disease.

The problem is not the use of the DAT scan. Nor is it the fact that the scan supported a degenerative parkinsonian syndrome. The problem is the timing and language. A scan obtained after the frame has already been installed can easily become confirmatory surplus rather than a true discriminator. “As expected” is not a neutral phrase. It shows that the result is being read through an already governing expectation.

Expectation is not always wrong. But when expectation follows premature finality, it becomes dangerous. It allows later evidence to stabilise a frame rather than test it.

This is the central epistemic mechanism of the case: uncertainty is recorded before closure, then confirmation is read through closure, then later contradiction is absorbed by closure.

By 2025, this mechanism is visible in the response to Liam’s complaint. Dr Monaghan’s letter is careful, apologetic, and humane. It accepts that Liam suffered and that the clinician did not understand how Liam felt. It explains that the belief and understanding remained idiopathic Parkinson’s disease. It acknowledges that Sinemet helps the experience of pain. It also explains caution around levodopa escalation in a younger patient because of dyskinesia and other risks.

The response is clinically coherent inside its own frame. That is precisely why it matters. It shows that a frame can be humane, reasoned, and still foreclosing. The patient’s later therapeutic contradiction is not denied; it is interpreted as compatible with the existing diagnosis. Sinemet helps the pain experience, but this does not necessarily make the pain a direct Parkinson’s phenomenon. Parkinson’s may magnify pain from other causes. The management caution remains justified by young-onset Parkinson’s risks. The diagnosis survives.

This is what happens when uncertainty loses authority. It remains available as explanation, but not as governance. It can explain why things were difficult, why choices were cautious, why the presentation was complex, why the patient’s pain was hard to place. But it cannot reopen the structure that gave the diagnosis power.

That is the wrong role for uncertainty.

Uncertainty should not merely excuse difficulty. It should regulate finality.

A system that knows it is uncertain must act differently from a system that knows it has completed inquiry. It may still treat. It may still support the patient. It may still use a working label. But it must build into the record the conditions of revision. It must avoid giving provisional conclusions the appearance of settled fact. It must not allow administrative mechanisms to outrun epistemic entitlement.

The phrase “epistemic entitlement” is useful here. It asks not simply whether a belief was reasonable, but whether the system had earned the right to let that belief govern. A clinician may reasonably suspect Parkinson’s disease. A team may reasonably avoid levodopa in a young patient. A hospital may reasonably discharge with a working plan. But the right to let that diagnosis become durable, certified, self-reinforcing, and complaint-resistant is a higher threshold.

This case is about that threshold.

The later St Vincent’s genetic confirmation does not prove that the threshold was obviously unmet in a simplistic way. It does not permit a smug retrospective argument that PRKN/PARK2 should have been certain in 2017. It does something more precise. It shows that the unresolved material recorded in the earlier file — young onset, dystonic features, foot-centred phenomenology, genetic suspicion, atypicality, and treatment-response ambiguity — was not noise. It was signal. The 2026 letter confirms two pathogenic Parkin variants and states that Liam’s Parkinsonism and dystonia are due to compound heterozygous mutations in the Parkin gene.

The significance is structural. The later result shows that the earlier uncertainty was real enough to matter. The question is not whether clinicians should have predicted the genetic answer. The question is why the uncertainty already visible in the record did not more effectively constrain the authority of the idiopathic frame.

This distinction protects the argument from hindsight bias. The claim is not: because we now know PRKN, they should have known PRKN. The claim is: because they did not know enough, the record should not have allowed one provisional explanatory structure to harden beyond its warrant.

That is a sequencing claim, not a retrospective correctness claim.

The phrase “uncertainty deprived of authority” also helps distinguish this case from ordinary diagnostic delay. Many diagnoses evolve. A first label may later be refined. A broad category may become a precise genetic subtype. That is not necessarily failure. The failure arises when the broad category becomes practically unrevisable before its unresolved features have been tracked, protected, and made capable of governing future reassessment.

In this case, the unresolved features did not disappear. The feet kept returning. Dystonia language kept appearing. Pain remained dominant. Medication response became increasingly important. The patient’s own concern intensified. Yet the inherited frame retained authority.

The problem was not uncertainty; the problem was certainty behaving as though uncertainty had been discharged.

This is why the case belongs not only to neurology but to health-data ethics. A record is not a passive memory. It is an active instrument of future interpretation. If a record turns uncertainty into background while turning a diagnosis into an administrative fact, later clinicians and administrators inherit an uneven field. The diagnosis arrives with authority. The uncertainty arrives as detail.

That imbalance matters. A later clinician reading “Parkinson’s Disease” in the primary-diagnosis position is not in the same epistemic posture as a clinician reading “working diagnosis: young-onset Parkinsonism; unresolved foot-centred dystonic pain; levodopa deferred; genetic uncertainty active; review required if medication response contradicts frame.” The first record closes. The second record guards openness.

The difference is not semantic. It is practical.

It affects medication decisions. It affects whether pain is treated as central or peripheral. It affects whether a patient is routed to a movement-disorder specialist. It affects whether a complaint is classified as clinical disagreement or governance problem. It affects whether data rectification is understood as a dispute with a diagnosis or a challenge to the completeness of the record. It affects whether future contradiction appears disruptive enough to matter.

Uncertainty, properly recorded, should create obligations.

It should require explicit review points. It should require explanation to the patient of what remains unresolved. It should require restraint in statutory certification or, where certification is necessary for access, explicit qualification. It should require that later inconsistent evidence be treated as potentially frame-altering, not merely as additional complexity. It should require governance processes to distinguish clinical judgment from the administrative consequences of clinical judgment.

This case shows what happens when that does not occur. Uncertainty becomes literary rather than operational. It enriches the record without controlling it. It makes the correspondence look nuanced without making the system behave cautiously about finality.

That is a dangerous form of sophistication. A crude record that simply says “Parkinson’s disease” may be easier to criticise. A sophisticated record that says “Parkinson’s disease, but atypical, complex, possibly genetic, with unresolved foot and leg symptoms, with levodopa deferred, with future regret possible” is ethically more difficult. It seems to disclose the uncertainty. But if it then proceeds as though the uncertainty has no authority over finality, the sophistication becomes part of the containment.

The record knows, but the system acts as though it does not know that it knows.

That is one of the deepest features of the case. Knowledge appears everywhere: the feet, atypicality, dystonic gait, youth, genetic possibility, medication caution, patient concern, later Sinemet response. The failure is not lack of data. It is failure of governance over what the data should be allowed to do.

In a public archive, this essay should therefore resist dramatic simplification. It should not accuse the record of being empty. It should not accuse clinicians of never recognising complexity. It should not claim that uncertainty was hidden. The exact opposite is more powerful.

The uncertainty was there.

It was written.

It was shared.

It was preserved.

It just was not obeyed.
