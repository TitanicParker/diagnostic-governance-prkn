Source extract 1

Neurology clinic Dear [GP], It was a pleasure to see [Patient] back at clinic today. [Patient] explains he is getting on very well. He’d bought a dog this time last year. He feels his physical activity is now much better. He is walking 2.5-3 hrs per day. He finished his qualification as a mediator and is due to qualify. He is starting another course presently and he will be doing a healing practitioner course. Most mornings the feet take 20-25 mins to warm up. They are a bit sore - with the corns and calluses feeling like a “coronal pain” around the edges. He wonders is it neuralgic? He has cramping but it is almost “zero percent”. There have been maybe just 3-4 of small finger locking crampings in the last few months. The Meds are above. Small Sinemets are optional. He takes some Diazepam in lieu of the missing Rivotril, but that will be back soon. He was not taking rivotril every night anyway He has to adjust Sinemet according to expected demands e.g. for the playing darts. In terms of other issues He has lost a load of weight. This is with the dog and with intention. Sleep is generally OK. Bowels are regular and he is going every day. Alcohol intake is about 4 pints every 2nd day and he feels this is much more under control. If he went over 4 pints he would not be able to manage it as [Patient] explained. Impression. Things are going well here. Looking at [Patient] today, it is at most "subtle" in terms of excess movement. I can see it because "I am looking for it". It is not intrusive and it is not excessive. However there are very subtle elements of slightly excessive movement. In no way would it be fair to characterise this as ordinary wiggling dyskinesia but there is just that slight little excessive movement. Nevertheless, it is at a very acceptable level and it certainly is not intrusive; embarrassing nor bothersome for him. However it has to be remembered that this is happening with a very minimal dose of levodopa built into that Stalevo and he had more difficulty with Stalevo 75 whereby he well remembers being very obviously dyskinetic (the chorea). Recommendations. It will only be a short period of time before Rivotril comes back available. These are supply chain issues that are plaguing medicine at the moment. He actually has lots and lots of Rivotril at home. His Rivotril usage was may be half the days per month at a maximum. In a similar way his usage of the diazepam (that he has been given to supplement the loss of the Rivotril has been pretty minimal). For technical reasons, if he wants to take a benzodiazepine at night to assist with sleep; I would really prefer it would be the Rivotril. There are a range of reasons for this. The diazepam is very short acting. I would just prefer that it would be the Rivotril that would be used as it is safer. This is only if he needs it. We have had a long chat about how things are likely to change over time with dyskinesia. Most people will prefer the “experience” of dyskinesia as troublesome as it is , as opposed to being the frozen or akinetic unable to move. We had a long chat about the logic in previous considerations of Sinemet usages. 50% of all people on Sinemet will have dyskinesia within 5 years. But delayed start studies and other work proves that while the Sinemet fuels or power the dyskinesia that it does not “cause” it. The cause is the impaired ability of the brain in handling normal dopamine. While this may give more trouble in time to come with increased dopamine that may become needed - - as above most individuals will “prefer” the excessive movement over being akinetically rigid. He had done chemistry at college and [Patient] asked about the way different chemical compounds and indeed pesticides can maybe be associated with inducing Parkinson's disease? He would have taken a smell of certain compounds at various different times. The short answer here is that it is really, really difficult to see or imagine how he could have chemically induced his own Parkinson's. When we see it, really the only place we see it happening in usual society is very rarely certain "drug dealers" involved in the compounding of certain agents, developing this sort of Parkinsonian scenario. It is unlikely that his is a toxic Parkinsonism. Obviously his own situation is very different to the usual drug induced Parkinson's that we also see from medicinal compounds such as in the management of schizophrenias. [Patient] asked how far away from a cure. He will forgive me please for being "realistic". Unfortunately things are still a very long way away. Our biggest hopes and expectations had centred majorly on antibody therapy-antusynculein nuclear antibody therapy. 2 major trials were published in the summer and unfortunately these did not go well at all (Editorial from NEJM attached). Basically the treatment failed in terms of clearing the pathological substrate of Parkinson's disease – the synuclein. Nevertheless, while it is not meant to be a consolation, the very fact of the magnitude of the population that are affected by Parkinson's disease will always ensure that there will always be massive research into this on an ongoing basis. I have not made any changes today. Follow-up: In 1 year Neurology Clinic. I do hope this is of assistance. If you have any questions or concerns please do not hesitate to contact me. Thank you, Yours sincerely, [Consultant Neurologist]
