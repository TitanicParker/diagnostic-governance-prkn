April 2018-04-24th [Patient] finds concentration is good. He can play at a really, really high level “Countdown” on an ongoing basis. He continues doing this online - as the best over-30 player online, in the world But he’ll very “easily loose train of thought and when the concentration is gone - - it’s gone”. [Patient]’s main concerns are physical: He is having foot cramps and hand cramps. It is nearly always the LEFT hand. Two weeks ago , while walking up the stairs, he pulled a muscle in the RIGHT chest. He has had a crick in the neck and shoulder e.g. on stretching to look for a car parking space. The pain and discomfort is worse than he’d expect. He knows that this should only last a day or two – but it can get to him for c 2-3 weeks. It is a “ball of pain” that moves. [Patient] is very cold sensitive. Hand swing is good. He feels that the gait is a bit off – he lacks both “fluency and speed”, for someone who he feels was good beforehand. The relief from tension is fantastic “It’s nice to be able to walk 20metres” as he says “without tension”. Transitions on floor surfaces are now OK e.g. going from carpet to tiles. Sleep is good. Movement in bed is as good as ever it was. Anxiety has subsided. Toe supports on both feet are a help . Nails are pared back. Bowels are “right as rain”. Movements are fairly uniform and they are formed - - he is NOT having to push. Two-and-a-half weeks ago he went to his general practitioner with a chest infection or at least a cough of 2 different semiologies. He was given reflux medications at that stage. [Movement Disorder Specialist] had explained why he would probably find it easier to stop smoking than other people. The smoking is 11-12 per day and he knows we would be very keen on him trying to stop. Vision is “excellent”, except – it’s more work than it used to be - - it’s like he has focus and concentrate. He put on weight - - - but he is not bothered about this. I was a bit bothered though initially, but it is very stable and he’s happier at this level of weight currently. Rivotril - - he has pains in upper back and pains in the neck - - - He doesn't know the dose but by the description I think it is 0.5 mg. He would get 14 tablets a month and [Patient] will take a half tablet - - - he’s had 35 in the last 9 months – he will take a half of the 0.5mg. He’ll sometimes use it for sleep. It really helps the neck and back by the morning – and he’ll be better the next day for having taken it the night before. =============== Our Nurse [Parkinson's Nurse] had suggested stopping Procyclidne for two weeks in Sept /Oct 2017 (regarding the blurring of the vision – [Patient] had seen me that day), but when he did that - - the first thing he noticed changing and changing rapidly was the return on the foot, of the outside left corn. Symptoms got worse a bit. (He remains on the Azilect) But he was more “shifty” “unrelaxed” off the Proclyidine, He had been off of it for a full two weeks. He returned to Ailect at 2.5mg TDS-QDS --------------------------- I’m delighted to note he is back at work in Shipping with Integer Medical. He feels he’s doing well with the physical task but also in a partial Health and Safety role too, and the cognitive aspects of it. He summarises it in one important statement – “I enjoy being alive.” He feels he does have a bit of irritability – (but he seems fine to me.) [Patient] has some probably piriformis pain on the left side. It’s about 1x per week for a couple of hours. He does vary the Procylidine a bit, this is unintentionally – and if he does miss it he will feel tighter in the left bottom/? Piriformis later. .================ Current Rx: 1 x 1mg Azilect 4 x ½ half tablets of Procylidine i.e ½ of the 5mg = 2.5mg QDS (he is varying this a bit) Co Q10 - - - he takes about 200mg per day. He is off the PPi currently – it didn’t anything much for the recent cough. =============== Exam note: [Patient] actually tends to move a little while he is sitting in the chair. It is not dyskinetic as such-and it is more than would be customarily expected in the hypokinetic disorder that it iPD. Obviously it is probably much better than him being frozen and bradykinetic. I certainly don't think this is an " overtreatment" phenomenology" in that it reflects the improvement in tension that he describes, and [Patient] describes that he was "always fidgety". We did a video of him today both of the hand movements and also the gait. There is bradykinesia however it is not bad. The walk is actually very good. He has a good arm swing without having to concentrate on it at all. Certainly the left-hand occasionally does "collide" with the leg but it is really relatively excellent. ================ Impression Obviously he could be very much better if we would actually give him the levodopa or another dopamine agonist ---however for a very clearly set out reasons, we do not want to do that – at least for quite some time. He probably isn't going to be very tolerant of being given more and more procyclidine given the visual issues previously with it. He is still keen that we might at some stage increase the Procyclidine and we will try sooner or later - - probably increasing one timing at a time. Incidentally, while our overarching interest with procyclidine is to avoid visual issues or cogntive difficulties, he was asking me as regards whether there will be cognitive consequences from being on the procyclidine? In point of fact if there are cognitive effects from procyclidine (and younger people tolerate it generally very well), they are pure "acute or transient effects" i.e. it is not doing any cognitive damage to him whatsoever. As regards the Rivotril, in other circumstances we would be deeply concerned about this. However it is relatively infrequent and he is not taking an excessive amount of it. It is relatively long acting and therefore it has are much less addictive potential. It controls very well aspect of his Parkinsons There is another particular justification for this: It relates to the pains and cramps and spasms and that "ball of pain" in the neck that can go down the back. [Patient] will have the ordinary aches and pains and “cricks” that all 40-year-olds have. However in the last 10 years it has begun to be significantly understood that people with Parkinson’s have a much more "magnified" experience of pain than everybody else. Therefore what he describes is not unreasonable or unusual - although it sounds different to lots of other 40-year-olds, who might have some degree of degenerative disc changes: Because the Rivotril relaxes this to a certain degree, I think it is reasonable to let him have it in much the way he is taking. In simple terms we are much more willing to tolerate in him then we will are in other people. The simple reason for this is because cognitively even though he describes how he can lose his train of thought, he is functioning at a particularly very high level. I was initially a bit concerned about the weight but he is not worried. I would just ask them to keep a weekly record of the weight because I do not want it heavier than he is at the moment. Recommendations As detailed above (I forgot to discuss running the Genetics with [Patient] today I do apologise --- – but he’ll have a copy of this at home and I’ll explain everything here: Obviously I’d be particularly interested to know – but this is certainly not about satisfying my wanting to know: I sort of want to delay it - - but basically if at any time he wants [Patient] done – for any reason - we will run it. But there is a reasonable logic to doing the isolated test for PARK2 PRKN/or the 30 gene panel now (and indeed Prof Healy would be happy with that approach) , if [Patient] would like? If he decides he wants it done at any time, if [Patient] might send me or [Parkinson's Nurse] a note – we’ll get it arranged.
