Neurology Clinic – Oct 2020-10-08th Dear [GP], It was a pleasure to see [Patient] back at neurology outpatient’s clinic today. In general physically [Patient] feels he is pretty good- all things considered. He knows there is always the risk of having an injury , but physically things are OK He can play darts and play well – and the game is good. In terms of the shoulder/arm it can tire and fatigue but it is OK. The severity of what he had gone through with the right shoulder this time last year is largely settled. Liram has had a bad corn on the outside of the LEFT foot but Bunion covers have been successful He is back on the bike which is great: [Patient] will be cycling wherever he needs to be going. Occasionally he’d be happy to head out as far as to Oranmore. He has actually lost weight which is likely the exercise and the lack of alcohol. As regards the Temprament. [Patient] finds himself impatient and “likely to express it”. He feels family would say that he has changed. They’d be reluctant to say he was a “bad person”. [Patient] feels he isn’t the healing presence he used to be. An example [Patient] gives in a chat with a family member wherein he was quite forthright in telling a family member about a quite worrying possibly diagnosis. Conversely he wonders is it simply an effect of lockdown. Subjectively he doesn’t feel depressed and subjectively he doesn’t feel anxious. Moreover he feels less anxious than he was before. Cognitively he doesn’t find the brain the best. This is a question obviously with the Kemadrin (which is also always a question with the vision). He described for example that he couldn’t remember an appointment date for a date already set in a consultation done yesterday. [Patient] sees a Psychologist for counselling, [GP] had wanted this done to work on insight into the “realisation” of Parkinsons. There is a headachy element too, which he isn’t sure what it’s from. Its at night time and it is at the very least mild most nights. It feels bitemporal and frontal. He actually had trouble putting words on it. It has a jangling quality. He won’t need to close eyes, and noise is minimally bothersome at a certain volume – even low. Smells don’t bother him (Olfaction is intact and he actually can identify it as a citrus smell, but although this is a relatively strong smell for me, he did not find it “overpowering”). Offering a list of words he wouldn’t pick throbbing or pounding but maybe pulsings. It doesn’t last more than a few minutes. There is a quality of tightness. Therefore, although it would not last more than a few minutes, it will recurrently happen over an evening. There is no specific lateralisation - to the right or to the left. It certainly does not waken him. He was never a Headachy person but [Patient] was car sick as a child, quite often. His Dad used to get Migraines. Cigarette smoking is approximately 12 or 14 rolled up cigarettes. And most of them are with some degree of cannabis mixed with tobacco. There is no family history of aneurysmal cerebral disease here. Alcohol is nothing at all since Jan 2020. He has been to Lanzarote a couple of times this year, and things went well. Exam is generally stable. There was a minimal excess of movement, but this was very, very little comparatively. I would have difficulty in knowing which was the lateralised side. While walking initially I would have thought it was the right upper limb however there was the appearance of a “throw” in the left lower limb. We got [Patient] jogging up and down the corridor which was actually quite okay and then looking at the walk again it does become clear that the arm swing issue is actually on the left. However all-in-all, despite the symptoms he has of excessive movement in the feet especially while turning, the walk quality is actually very good. Conversely, he is only 3 years into this diagnosis Coming back to the foot throw which looks a little bit like a foot drop-this is actually parkinsonian because the power for dorsiflexion, plantarflexion, inversion and eversion are all normal. Of note He would have an exquisite sensitivity of the feet, and he’ll always want the feet covered. There is excessive movement and also urge to move the feet. He doesn’t felt he balances great, but balance is fine in the bike – he thinks as he has momentum. Impression I am not sure what the headaches are. They do not conveniently sits with any particular diagnosis. There may be a slight migrainous element but by criteria he is not young enough to be given a migraine diagnosis notwithstanding the family history. Also really, typically migraine headaches do last longer. I think we would all just be happier if I was to do a CT brain but also with the CT angiogram. There is no family history of aneurysmal disease but we will check this out. It is probably a variant of a tension-type headache given its bilateral and episodic nature. One can never out rule that it might be coming from the Kemadrin procyclidine and the vision issues with that. Of note compared with other things he’s always been particularly of the opinion that the Azilect has been a help. Theoretically if in the future, and before we’d consider Sinemet (he is still only 42) if we offered him a low dose Neupro at 2mg or 4 mg again he would be interested in trying – despite everything that has gone before. Part of the problem at high dose was as per his experience an almost amphetamine quality of being stimulated in an artificial way at night. A tiny dose of Neupro might help the RLS of the legs. As regards the irritability the conventional thing is to offer him Escitaopram – but I’ll discuss this below. Recommendations Just as regards the headaches (because they are little unusual) we will actually arrange a CT brain but the real purpose of this is a CT angiogram. This means that he will need contrast dye. That dye contrast is utterly harmless - however when they inject it he will feel warm flushing sensation surging through his body and [Patient] may also have a sensation of feeling like he is wetting himself or even passing a bowel movement. It would be gone within 30-40 seconds and there will not have been an accident. We have had a long chat about neurodegeneration in Parkinson’s disease. I am not going to cover that all here. Basically the simple proposition is that [Patient] has a neurodegenerative disorder. A significant subset of people with Parkinson’s disease are going to get Parkinson’s disease dementia. I simply can’t guarantee this will not happen to him. He has significant worries and these are reasonable and appropriate worries. The age of onset he was at, was extremely young. It is simply absolutely and utterly impossible for us to predict with certainty his dementia risk. It would also be impossible to predict when he would get into trouble with dementia – IF EVER. At the current time I think a lot of his memory problems are from issues like the Kemadrin. Nevertheless that serves him well. The Kemadrin will never contribute to the development of a dementia state – in terms of “brain damage”. In fact none of the medications that he is on, would be contributory to the progression of a dementia state – even if agents like Kemadrin might contribute to day-to-day awarenesses of memory problems. Obviously the question becomes ---what can I do to reduce / limit my risk of dementia. There is no disease modifying therapies that we can offer. The best evidence in terms of lifestyle measures for dementing illnesses obviously predominantly comes from Alzheimer’s disease. I think it is fair to say it is the generally held view that the lifestyle measures that would protect him against dementia are believed to have a significant impact, and are as follows: A mixed varied diet with adequate fish/fish oils. Novelty in life and engagement with new pursuits and activities. Management of Depression/Anxiety , but there are not subjective features of either currently with [Patient], and not really other factors suggestive of that, other than this “irritability”. Not smoking which is something that really does need to be addressed here. The alcohol is now completely gone and that will be massively protective against this situation. I think it is fair to say that notions of “one drink per day being protective”, are not really accepted any longer. Even if there was any significant brian volume change on a current MRI we’d likely blame the alcohol as opposed to the iPD (Conversely other people can have excellent looking brains with iPD and still be very badly physically affected) His own particular cognitive background both in terms of his intellect, and also his language based interests will be hugely neuroprotective. (the point is that he could have massive changes in brain volume over a long life and still actually score cognitively excellently on Neuropscyhological testing based on his Cognitive Reserve which without exaggeration or flattery, should be genuinely excellent) As it happens I think it is necessary and wise to do the CT brain and the CT angiogram. If he truly wishes to understand whether there are neurodegenerative brain volume changes already, it is an easy matter to ask for an MRI of the brain. This for example can be compared with his 2017 MRI and comment can be sought. We do not do volumetric assessments at the current time but they are a future scanning modality that will be available on an automated basis over the next decade (they are mainly at research stages now and they are not commonly done). However we can “eyeball it” and we can give him an answer if [Patient] requires it. But it is an overly simplistic reduction to conceptulise dementing illnesses as purely a function of “brain volume”. It is actually about tract, connectivity and the loss of connections within the brain. While this can be reflected in brain volume, it is probably a poorer marker and in years to come we will probably use markers of connectivity and lack of connectivity as the real markers of dementia/predementia. Incidentally if we did have particular neurocognitive dementing concerns about [Patient] right now, we might actually be doing a PET scan - - but I don’t think its necessary either. Coming back to the temperament issue. I wondered whether we should consider something like an SSRI “antidepressant”. [Patient] does not feel depressed and he does not feel anxious but the question is whether the temperamental issues are an aspect of what is in essence anxiety (and I’ve explained how depression and anxiety are neurochemically viewed as different aspects or projections of the same neurochemical disorder). These symptoms would not be unreasonable in Parkinson’s disease and indeed they are common in iPD. Our standard first treatment is often citalopram or escitalopram. I think if he is becoming more bothered by this in his terms of his interactions with others or intrinsically how [Patient] feels himself - that e.g. Lexapro would be an option. However there has recently been considerable success here with weight loss. Certainly I’d like [Patient] to do a little bit better than he is with weight loss, however I am reluctant just at the moment to suggest Lexapro, because he is on a path of reducing weight. In addition, sometimes those SSRIs/SNRIs can be associated with erectile dysfunction in men. Nevertheless, if he is becoming more bothered about temperament or family members are becoming more bothered about temperament, Lexapro would be a very reasonable thing to try? Follow-up: 9-10 months Will review with ANP [Parkinson's Nurse] in interim if issues. I do hope this is of assistance. If you have any questions or concerns please do not hesitate to contact me. Thank you, Yours sincerely, [Consultant Neurologist]
